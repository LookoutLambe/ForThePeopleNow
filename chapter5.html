CHAPTER 5

Executive Orders & The Law of War

Fact-checking the claims about military authority and presidential continuity

Derek Johnson Doesn't Know What He's Talking About

Let's be blunt. Derek Johnson (via thedocuments.info and "1776 Nation") has built a following by throwing around military document numbers, executive order numbers, and legal citations. He claims the United States is under "military occupancy" and that Trump remains the real Commander in Chief through a secret legal framework.

It sounds impressive if you've never actually read the documents he cites. But when someone with a Master's in Emergency Management sits down and reads them? The errors aren't subtle. They're not matters of interpretation. He gets basic historical facts wrong. He confuses unrelated laws. He mischaracterizes one of the most important Supreme Court cases in American history.

This chapter walks through his biggest claims, side by side with what the actual documents say. You can read every source yourself — we've linked the PDFs below.

Derek Johnson's framework contains fundamental historical and legal errors. He conflates distinct legal instruments, mischaracterizes landmark Supreme Court cases, and misrepresents the timeline of military law. These are not matters of interpretation — they are factual errors that undermine his entire argument.

Error #1: Confusing Two Completely Different Laws

Johnson treats the National Emergencies Act (1976) and Executive Order 12148 (1979) as the same thing, using this to build his narrative about presidential emergency powers.
These are completely different legal instruments. The NEA was passed by Congress and signed by Gerald Ford in 1976. EO 12148 was signed by Jimmy Carter in 1979 and transferred emergency management to FEMA. Different authors, different purposes, different years.

Error #2: Getting Military Law History Wrong by 175 Years

Johnson implies the UCMJ predates the Declaration of Independence, suggesting an unbroken chain of military law supporting his framework.
The UCMJ was enacted by Congress in 1950. While military law existed since the Articles of War (1775), the UCMJ as a unified code is a 20th century product. Johnson is off by about 175 years. This isn't a minor quibble — it's a foundational error.

Error #3: Misrepresenting the Most Important Supreme Court Case in History

Johnson characterizes Marbury v. Madison (1803) as a case about the "appearance" of law, suggesting it supports the idea that things may not be as they legally appear.
Marbury v. Madison established judicial review — the Supreme Court's power to declare acts of Congress unconstitutional. Chief Justice Marshall's opinion established that the Constitution is the supreme law of the land. Johnson's characterization bears zero resemblance to the actual case. This is Constitutional Law 101.

Four Principal Claims Examined

1. Current U.S. Continuity of Government Status

Johnson claims the U.S. is operating under continuity of government protocols that effectively make Trump the real Commander in Chief. In reality, continuity of government (COG) plans exist for emergencies and disasters — they are standard operational readiness plans maintained by every administration. COG does not transfer power to a former president. The Presidential Succession Act and the 20th Amendment are clear on how presidential authority transfers. See Chapter 6 for detailed analysis of actual COG operations.

2. National Guard Federalization

Johnson claims National Guard activations prove a secret military operation under Trump. In reality, National Guard units are routinely activated for natural disasters, civil unrest, border support, and public health emergencies. Title 32 activations are under state governor authority; Title 10 activations bring Guard units under federal control. Neither proves a secret military operation. See The National Guard sub-page for details.

3. Secretary of Defense Authority Delegation

Johnson claims the delegation of certain authorities from the Secretary of Defense is evidence of a military plan. Authority delegation is a standard administrative procedure in any large organization. The DoD has published directives on delegation of authority since its inception. This is ordinary government operations, not evidence of a secret plan.

4. Law of War Manual Applicability

Johnson claims the DoD Law of War Manual applies to current domestic operations as evidence of military occupation. The Law of War Manual is a reference document for U.S. military personnel on the law of armed conflict — it governs conduct during wartime against foreign adversaries. It does not authorize or describe domestic military occupation of the United States. Applying it to domestic governance represents a fundamental misunderstanding of its purpose and scope.
