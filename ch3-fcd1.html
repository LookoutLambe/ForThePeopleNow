Federal Continuity Directive 1 (FCD-1)

The core framework requirements for continuity planning within the federal government

The History of Continuity Planning

Continuity of Operations (COOP) and Continuity of Government (COG) planning didn't appear out of nowhere, and it wasn't created by Donald Trump or any recent president. It has 70+ years of documented history rooted in Cold War nuclear preparedness.

1950s
Cold War Nuclear Preparedness
COG planning focused on preserving the presidential line of succession and relocating senior leaders to secure, alternative facilities outside Washington, DC, such as the Mount Weather Emergency Operations Center.
1988
Executive Order 12656 (Reagan)
Assigned national security emergency preparedness responsibilities to federal departments. Designated the NSC as the principal forum for continuity policy. Required every agency to identify essential functions and develop plans to maintain them.
1998
PDD-67 (Clinton)
Presidential Decision Directive 67 established the concepts of Enduring Constitutional Government (ECG), Continuity of Government (COG), and Continuity of Operations (COOP) as three distinct but interrelated planning streams.
2001
September 11 Attacks
After 9/11, federal buildings in DC were evacuated. Government resumed the next day, but agencies recognized that Cold War-era plans assuming advance warning were inadequate for sudden terrorist attacks. COOP planning was overhauled.
2003
EO 13286 (Bush)
Transferred continuity oversight functions to the new Department of Homeland Security, recognizing the post-9/11 threat environment.
2004
Federal Preparedness Circular 65
FEMA issued FPC-65, providing detailed COOP planning requirements for executive branch agencies. This became the precursor to FCD-1.
2007
NSPD-51/HSPD-20 (Bush)
Established a comprehensive national policy on continuity, defining National Essential Functions (NEFs) and requiring all executive departments to maintain COOP programs.
2012
FCD-1 First Published
The Federal Continuity Directive 1 was formally issued, replacing FPC-65 as the core framework document for federal continuity planning.
2016
PPD-40 (Obama)
Presidential Policy Directive 40 modernized continuity planning. Gave FEMA a leadership role in coordinating and overseeing continuity programs across the entire federal government.
2020
EO 13961 (Trump)
Strengthened governance of continuity programs and directed implementation of the Federal Mission Resilience Strategy (FMRS). Every executive department had to implement FMRS into their continuity programs.
2024
FCD-0 (Internal)
A new foundational directive, referred to internally as FCD-0, was developed to enhance and clarify the FCD-1 and FCD-2 framework. This was the direct result of EO 13961's requirements.
Why this timeline matters: Jon Herold claims EO 13961 was "proof of devolution," a secret plan by Trump to transfer power. In reality, EO 13961 resulted in the government strengthening its continuity framework with FMRS and eventually FCD-0. It did exactly what it said: enhanced emergency management across the executive branch. The exact opposite of what he claimed.

COOP vs. COG: Understanding the Difference

Grifters constantly confuse these two concepts, or deliberately blur them. They are related but distinct:

COOPCOG
Continuity of OperationsContinuity of Government
Focuses on maintaining essential functionsFocuses on preserving government leadership
Staff, facilities, technology, dataPresidential succession, constitutional governance
Every agency develops its own COOP planGovernment-wide coordination
Can be activated for routine disruptions (weather, fire, IT failure)Activated for catastrophic events threatening constitutional government
Led by individual agency headsOverseen by NSC, FEMA, and White House
"COOP and COG are designed to ensure survival of a constitutional form of government and the continuity of essential federal functions." — Congressional Research Service Report RL31857
Both COOP and COG exist to preserve the Constitution, not to override it. Every continuity plan, at every level, is designed to maintain constitutional governance during emergencies. That is the literal opposite of what devolution theorists claim.

FCD-1: The Core Framework

FCD-1 operates under PPD-40 authority and was developed by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and FEMA. It establishes the core framework requirements for continuity planning within the federal government, with specific focus on the executive branch.

Under FCD-1, FEMA is authorized to train, exercise, and implement continuity planning across all federal departments and agencies. The directive defines:

National Essential Functions (NEFs)

The most critical functions that must be maintained at all times. These are defined at the national level and drive all subordinate continuity planning.

Primary Mission Essential Functions (PMEFs)

Agency-specific functions that directly support the NEFs. Each department identifies its own PMEFs based on its statutory mission.

Mission Essential Functions (MEFs)

The broader set of functions an agency must maintain during an emergency. These include but extend beyond the PMEFs.

Orders of Succession

Who takes over when leaders are incapacitated. Every agency must have documented succession plans at least 3 deep for key positions.

Delegation of Authority

Legal documentation specifying who can make decisions and take actions when primary officials are unavailable.

Alternate Facilities

Pre-identified locations where operations can be relocated. Must have communications infrastructure, security, and life support capabilities.

FCD-2: The Implementation Guide

FCD-2 is where the rubber meets the road. While FCD-1 tells you what to plan for, FCD-2 tells you how to do it. It provides actionable steps and procedures that agencies use to build and maintain their programs:

  • Plan development templates and formatting standards
  • Exercise requirements, including tabletop, functional, and full-scale
  • Training programs for continuity personnel
  • Assessment criteria for evaluating program readiness
  • Reporting requirements to FEMA

The Federal Mission Resilience Strategy (FMRS)

EO 13961 (December 2020) directed implementation of the Federal Mission Resilience Strategy. This wasn't a secret signal. It was a government-wide mandate to modernize how agencies approach continuity.

Every executive government department, from the Department of Energy to the Bureau of Reclamation, had to implement FMRS into their continuity programs. This happened before the 2024 FCD update even came out. The FMRS became the operational standard that agencies were already following.

This is what Jon Herold doesn't understand: EO 13961 wasn't a secret activation of devolution. It was a directive to improve emergency management. Every agency in the executive branch implemented it. The author of this site has seen it firsthand because this is what emergency management professionals do for a living.

FCD-0: The 2024 Enhancement

In 2024, the continuity framework was further strengthened with what was internally referred to as FCD-0, a foundational directive that enhanced and clarified what FCD-1 and FCD-2 had established. This was the direct result of the governance improvements mandated by EO 13961.

FCD-0 didn't replace FCD-1 and FCD-2. Instead, it provided the overarching framework that tied everything together. Think of it as the capstone document that formalized the Federal Mission Resilience Strategy into the continuity directive system.

The progression from EO 13961 → FMRS implementation → FCD-0 proves that the executive order Jon Herold claimed was "devolution" actually resulted in exactly what it said it would do: a stronger, clearer, better-organized federal continuity framework. Not a secret military takeover. Professional emergency management.
