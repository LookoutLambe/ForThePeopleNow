CHAPTER 8

The 1871 Act & Sovereign Citizen Theory

Debunking the “Two Constitutions” conspiracy

The Claim: "America Became a Corporation in 1871"

You've probably seen this one on social media: someone confidently states that in 1871, the United States secretly became a corporation, that there are "two constitutions," and that everything since then has been an elaborate lie perpetrated by shadowy banking interests.

It sounds wild. That's because it is. It's completely, provably false. And here's the part that most people sharing this don't know: this theory originated from a white supremacist movement.

What the 1871 Act Actually Is

The District of Columbia Organic Act of 1871 is one of the most boring pieces of legislation in American history. Seriously. It reorganized the local government of Washington D.C., combining the separate governments of Washington, Georgetown, and the surrounding areas into one unified territorial government.

That's it. It was an administrative restructuring of a city government. It didn't touch the U.S. Constitution. It didn't change the national government. It didn't create a corporation that replaced America.

The word "corporation" in the Act refers to a municipal corporation, which is the legal term for a city or town government. Your hometown is a municipal corporation. It doesn't mean it's owned by Wall Street. It means it has a charter that lets it govern itself.

The FBI has identified the sovereign citizen movement as a domestic terrorist threat due to their extreme beliefs and propensity for violence. When you share "1871" posts on social media, you're spreading a theory that comes from this movement.

Where This Theory Really Comes From

This is the part they don't tell you on TikTok.

The "1871 theory" traces directly back to the Posse Comitatus movement, a white supremacist group founded in the early 1970s by William Potter Gale, a man known for his racist, antisemitic, and anti-government views. The group believed county sheriffs were the supreme legal authority and that the federal government was illegitimate.

The "sovereign citizen" movement grew out of this, adopting the idea that the federal government had been secretly replaced by a corporate entity. They latched onto the 1871 Act because it used the word "corporation," ignoring that it meant "city government," not "business corporation."

Legal scholars, including Kermit Roosevelt from the University of Pennsylvania, have thoroughly debunked this interpretation. Politifact rated it False. The Southern Poverty Law Center tracks sovereign citizens as an extremist ideology. And yet, it keeps spreading on social media because it sounds like it could be true to someone who hasn't looked into it.

How Q Followers Recycled a White Supremacist Theory

In one of the more ironic turns in conspiracy theory history, followers of Q, many of whom consider themselves patriots, adopted a theory created by white supremacists and ran with it. Some even predicted that Trump would be re-inaugurated as "the 19th president" on March 4, 2021, based on the idea that every president since 1871 was illegitimate.

March 4, 2021 came and went. Nothing happened. But instead of abandoning the theory, they just moved the goalposts, because that's what conspiracy theories do.

Purpose

Prior to 1871, Washington D.C. was governed through a complicated system of locally elected officials, federally appointed commissions, and Congressional oversight. The Organic Act aimed to streamline the territorial government.

Provisions

  • Revoked individual charters of Washington and Georgetown
  • Combined them into a single territorial government
  • Established new government structure (governor, legislative assembly, board of public works)
  • Congress retained overriding authority and veto power

Impact

  • Centralized governance under one unified territorial government
  • Led to period of political corruption and excessive spending
  • Congress abolished the territorial government in 1874 and reverted to direct Congressional control

The Real History: Post-Civil War Reconstruction

To understand why this Act was passed, you need to understand what was happening in America in 1871. It wasn't a corporate takeover. It was Reconstruction:

1865

Abolishes slavery throughout the United States.
1868
14th Amendment
Grants citizenship to all persons born in the U.S., overturning Dred Scott.
1870
15th Amendment
Forbids denial of voting rights based on race.
1870-71
Enforcement Acts & KKK Act

1871
District of Columbia Organic Act
Reorganizes D.C. governance as part of the broader Reconstruction legislative effort. That's it. No corporate takeover. No second Constitution.
The 1871 Act was part of Reconstruction, the effort to rebuild America after the Civil War and secure rights for freed slaves. Claiming it was a secret corporate takeover ignores the actual historical context and, ironically, comes from a movement rooted in white supremacy that opposed these very reforms.

What the Internet Did

The sovereign citizen theory, the devolution theory, and the law of war claims are all symptoms of the same disease: the internet turned misinformation into an industry.

Before social media, sovereign citizen ideology was confined to fringe militia groups and white supremacist newsletters. The internet did three things:

  1. Stripped the white supremacist branding. The 1871 theory was repackaged without the racist origins, making it palatable to patriot communities who would have rejected it if they knew where it came from.
  2. Made it profitable. Content creators discovered that conspiracy theories drive engagement. More engagement means more subscribers, more donations, more ad revenue. Truth became secondary to clicks.
  3. Created echo chambers. Platforms like Telegram, TruthSocial, and Rumble allow these theories to circulate unchallenged. Anyone who pushes back gets banned from the group. The result: millions of Americans consuming misinformation in sealed information environments.

Devolution, the law of war theory, and sovereign citizen ideology are just a few examples of what the internet has done to our nation. Well-meaning patriots have been led astray by content creators who profit from their confusion. The antidote isn't more conspiracy theories. It's actual education, verified sources, and the willingness to question anyone who claims to have secret knowledge, especially when they're charging you for it.
