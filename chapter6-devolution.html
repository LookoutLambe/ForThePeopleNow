CHAPTER 6 — SUB-PAGE

True Devolution — What Real Devolution Planning Looks Like

A professional, documented, civilian-led process within the continuity of operations framework

What Is Devolution?

Devolution planning is "a critical component of an organization's continuity of operations (COOP) program" that enables "the ability to transfer statutory authority and responsibilities to alternate personnel and facilities."

In emergency management, devolution is a structured, professional process — not a secret military operation or a conspiracy theory. It is one component of the broader continuity of operations framework.

Five Main Objectives

  1. Maintaining Essential Functions — ensuring that critical government operations continue without interruption
  2. Enhancing Resilience — building organizational capacity to withstand disruptions
  3. Ensuring Leadership Continuity — maintaining clear chains of command and succession
  4. Protecting Personnel and Assets — safeguarding people and resources during emergencies
  5. Maintaining Operational Capability — preserving the ability to perform mission-critical tasks

Seven Key Elements

1. Identification of Essential Functions

Organizations must identify and prioritize their essential functions through function analysis and interdependency mapping. This ensures that the most critical operations receive resources first during a disruption.

2. Delegation of Authority

Clearly defining who assumes key roles when primary personnel are unavailable. This includes documented orders of succession and pre-delegated authorities.

3. Selection and Preparation of Devolution Personnel

Alternate personnel must be identified, trained, and prepared through training, exercises, and cross-training programs to ensure they can perform essential functions when called upon.

4. Establishment of Alternate Facilities

Pre-identified alternate locations must be equipped and ready to support relocated operations, including necessary infrastructure, communications, and security.

5. Communication and Information Management

Redundant communications systems must be established to ensure connectivity between primary and alternate sites, leadership, and partner organizations.

6. Resource Management

Planning for the financial, logistical, and human resources needed to sustain operations at alternate sites for extended periods.

7. Reconstitution Procedures

Recovery planning and post-event analysis procedures ensure that organizations can return to normal operations in an orderly manner once the emergency has passed.

Development Process

Devolution plans are developed through a systematic process:

  • Risk and Vulnerability Assessments — identifying threats and organizational weaknesses
  • Creating Detailed Procedures — documenting step-by-step processes for activation and operations
  • Training and Exercises — regularly practicing devolution procedures to ensure readiness
  • Reviewing and Revising Plans — continuously updating plans based on lessons learned and changing conditions

Historical Case Studies

Hurricane Katrina (2005)

Multiple federal agencies activated devolution plans in response to Hurricane Katrina. While the plans ensured continuity of essential functions, the response also revealed significant challenges in coordination, communication, and resource allocation that led to improvements in subsequent planning cycles.

September 11, 2001

The Department of Defense activated devolution plans on 9/11, relocating personnel to alternate sites to ensure continuity of military command and control. This real-world activation demonstrated both the value of devolution planning and the need for regular exercises and updates.

This is what real devolution looks like — a professional, documented, civilian-led process within the continuity of operations framework. It bears no resemblance to the conspiracy theory version promoted online.
