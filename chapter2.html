CHAPTER 2

The Military Oath

Why the oath of enlistment binds service members to the Constitution above all

The Oath Every Service Member Takes

Before we get into devolution, executive orders, or any of the theories floating around online, we need to talk about something fundamental — the oath that every single member of the United States military takes on day one.

"I do solemnly swear that I will support and defend the Constitution of the United States against all enemies, foreign and domestic; that I will bear true faith and allegiance to the same."

Read that again. It doesn't say "I swear to defend the President." It doesn't say "I swear to follow whatever orders come down." It says the Constitution. That's not an accident. The Founding Fathers, who were deeply distrustful of standing armies, designed it that way on purpose.

Why This Destroys the Devolution Theory

Here's where this gets really important for everything we discuss on this site. The devolution theory and the law of war claims both require the military to act outside the Constitution — to secretly take control of civilian government, to recognize a former president as the real commander-in-chief, to operate in the shadows against the established order.

That's the exact opposite of what every service member swears to do.

On January 12, 2021, the entire Joint Chiefs of Staff — the highest-ranking military officers in the country — issued an unprecedented memo making this crystal clear. They stated that Biden would be inaugurated as the 46th Commander in Chief "in accordance with the Constitution." They didn't hedge. They didn't use coded language. They told the world exactly where the military stands: with the Constitution.

If the military were secretly operating under a devolution plan, the Joint Chiefs just committed treason by publicly pledging allegiance to the "wrong" commander-in-chief. That didn't happen. Because devolution isn't real.

What the Oath Actually Requires

The Constitution Comes First — Always

When a soldier takes the oath, they acknowledge one supreme authority: the Constitution. Not a political party. Not a president. Not a general. The document itself. This is what makes the American military unique in the world — they serve a set of principles, not a person. Any theory that requires the military to act outside constitutional processes is asking them to violate their most sacred commitment.

Your Rights Are Non-Negotiable

The First Amendment guarantees your freedom of speech, religion, press, and assembly. The Second Amendment protects your right to bear arms. These aren't suggestions — they're constitutional guarantees that soldiers swear to protect. Any scenario where the Constitution is "suspended" — even temporarily, even for a good reason — puts every single one of your rights on the chopping block. That's not patriotism. That's the opposite.

The Three Branches Exist for a Reason

Congress makes the laws. The President enforces them. The courts interpret them. This system of checks and balances was designed to prevent exactly the kind of power concentration that conspiracy theories describe. When someone tells you the military secretly took over civilian authority, they're describing the destruction of this system — not its defense.

Soldiers Can Refuse Unconstitutional Orders

Here's something the conspiracy theorists never mention: soldiers aren't just allowed to refuse unconstitutional orders — they're required to. The oath includes obedience to the President and officers, but that obedience has a hard limit: the Constitution. Article II requires the President himself to swear to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution." An order to suspend it, circumvent it, or operate outside it is, by definition, unlawful. And every soldier knows it.

What the Military Actually Did After 2020

Actions speak louder than theories. Here's what actually happened:

  • January 12, 2021: The Joint Chiefs issued a memo recognizing Biden as the incoming commander-in-chief and condemning the January 6th violence as unlawful.
  • January 20, 2021: The military participated in the inauguration, provided security, and recognized the peaceful transfer of power exactly as the Constitution prescribes.
  • Every day since: No military unit has refused orders from the Biden administration. No general has publicly or privately indicated any secret parallel command structure exists.

If devolution were real — if the military were secretly operating under Trump's command — we would have seen some evidence by now. A leaked memo. A whistleblower. A single serving officer breaking ranks. Instead: nothing. Because there's nothing to reveal.

Honoring Those Who Served

Every veteran who has ever served — from the Revolution to Afghanistan — took an oath to the Constitution. Many gave their lives defending it. When internet personalities tell you the Constitution has been secretly suspended or that the military is operating outside it, they're disrespecting every service member who ever raised their right hand.

The Constitution doesn't need to be rescued by a secret military operation. It needs to be understood, defended, and used by citizens who participate in the system of self-governance it established.

The military oath is not a slogan. It's a binding commitment to the Constitution — not to any individual, party, or secret plan. Every conspiracy theory that requires the military to act outside constitutional authority is asking service members to break their most sacred promise. That's not patriotism. That's the opposite.
