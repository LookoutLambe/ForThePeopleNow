CHAPTER 7

National Response Framework & Emergency Management

How the government actually responds to emergencies

Why This Chapter Exists

Conspiracy theorists love to throw around terms like "emergency management," "continuity of operations," and "national response framework" as if they're secret codes. They're not. Emergency management is an actual profession with graduate programs, certifications, professional associations, and decades of institutional knowledge.

I know because I have a Master's degree in it. And when I hear people like Jon Herold and Derek Johnson misuse these terms, it's like watching someone who's never been to medical school try to perform surgery based on a YouTube video.

This chapter explains how emergency management actually works, so you can see for yourself that the processes described in conspiracy theories bear zero resemblance to reality.

What is Emergency Management?

Emergency management is the discipline of preparing for, responding to, and recovering from disasters. It's been a formalized field since the 1980s. It involves coordination between government agencies, nonprofits, businesses, and communities. It's not a secret operation; it's a transparent, professional practice with published standards and publicly available training courses.

The Four Phases (This Is EM 101)

Every emergency management professional learns these four phases on day one. If someone is talking about emergency operations and doesn't mention these, they don't know what they're talking about:

1. Preparedness

Planning before disaster strikes. Writing emergency plans, conducting training exercises, stockpiling supplies, establishing communication systems. This is where COOP and COG planning lives, and it's what Jon Herold misinterprets as "devolution."

2. Mitigation

Reducing risk before it becomes a disaster. Building codes, flood zones, infrastructure hardening. The boring but essential work that saves lives.

3. Response

The active phase when disaster hits. Deploying first responders, activating emergency operations centers, coordinating resources. This is where FEMA, the National Guard, and other agencies step in, always under civilian authority.

4. Recovery

Rebuilding after the crisis. Restoring infrastructure, providing assistance to affected communities, and documenting lessons learned for next time.

These four phases are the foundation of everything the government does in emergencies. Conspiracy theorists who claim to understand government emergency operations but can't describe these phases are telling you they haven't done their homework.

The Five Mission Areas

Under the National Preparedness Goal, emergency management is organized into five mission areas. Every emergency management professional knows these. They form the backbone of all federal, state, and local preparedness:

🛡

Prevention

Preventing an imminent act of terrorism or stopping a threat. Intelligence sharing, interdiction operations, and screening.

🛡

Protection

Protecting citizens, critical infrastructure, and key resources. Cybersecurity, physical security, border security.

🛡

Mitigation

Reducing the impact of disasters before they happen. Building codes, flood insurance, infrastructure hardening, community resilience.

🛡

Response

Saving lives, protecting property, stabilizing the community during and immediately after a disaster. Search and rescue, medical surge, mass care.

🛡

Recovery

Rebuilding infrastructure, restoring services, and helping communities return to normal. Long-term housing, economic recovery, health services.

The National Response Framework (NRF)

The National Response Framework is the guide for how the nation responds to all types of disasters and emergencies. It's built on scalable, flexible, and adaptable concepts that enable the whole community to work together.

Key principles of the NRF:

  • Engaged partnership: Leaders at all levels develop shared goals and work together
  • Tiered response: Incidents are managed at the lowest jurisdictional level possible. Local first, then state, then federal
  • Scalable operations: The response scales up or down based on the size and complexity of the incident
  • Unity of effort: Everyone works toward common objectives through unified command
  • Readiness to act: Effective response requires trained personnel and clear plans before an incident
Notice what's NOT in the NRF: secret military operations, presidential emergency action documents activating shadow governments, or the military taking over civilian authority. The NRF is entirely civilian-led with military support when requested through proper channels (see DSCA).

National Incident Management System (NIMS)

NIMS provides a standardized approach to incident management that all responders use, from a local house fire to a nationwide pandemic. It includes:

  • Incident Command System (ICS): The management structure used at every incident. Unified command, clear chain of command, manageable span of control. This is taught to every first responder in America.
  • Emergency Operations Centers (EOCs): Coordination facilities where agencies come together to support the response. Every county, state, and the federal government has one.
  • Multiagency Coordination Systems: How multiple organizations work together across jurisdictions.
  • Public Information Systems: Coordinated messaging through Joint Information Centers.

ICS was developed in the 1970s after catastrophic wildfires in California exposed coordination failures. It's been refined over 50 years and is now the standard worldwide. Every emergency management professional is trained in ICS. Jon Herold and Derek Johnson show no evidence of understanding it.

Why This Matters for Debunking Conspiracy Theories

Here's why this chapter exists on a site about devolution and law of war theories:

When Jon Herold talks about "continuity of government operations," he's describing a fantasy that has no connection to how emergency management actually works. When Derek Johnson claims the military is operating under secret protocols, he's ignoring the entire professional discipline that actually manages government operations during crises.

The real system is:

  • Civilian-led, always. Military supports when requested through DSCA.
  • Transparent. The NRF, NIMS, FIOPs, and FCDs are all publicly available.
  • Professional. Managed by trained emergency managers with degrees, certifications, and years of experience.
  • Constitutional. Every framework explicitly requires maintaining separation of powers and rule of law.
  • Documented. After-action reports, exercise evaluations, and improvement plans are standard practice.
Emergency management isn't a conspiracy; it's a career field. The author of this site has a Master's degree in it. The people who promote devolution and law of war theories have no training in it whatsoever. When they use EM terminology, they are using words they don't understand to describe processes they've never been trained in. That's not research. That's cosplay.

Family Emergency Preparedness

Emergency preparedness is not just a government responsibility. It starts with individuals and families. Local officials and relief workers will respond to disasters, but they may not be able to reach everyone for 12 to 72 hours. That is why every family should have an emergency plan, a 72-hour supply kit, and knowledge of what to do before, during, and after an emergency.
