CHAPTER 6 — SUB-PAGE

Federal Continuity Directive 1 (FCD-1)

The core framework requirements for continuity planning within the federal government

Quick History of Continuity Planning

Continuity of Operations (COOP) and Continuity of Government (COG) planning originated during the Cold War, when the threat of nuclear conflict demanded that the federal government develop plans to survive and continue functioning. Key early developments include:

  • Mount Weather Emergency Operations Center — established as a classified relocation site for federal leadership
  • NSDD 188 — a National Security Decision Directive that established foundational continuity planning frameworks

Executive Order 12656 (1988)

Signed by President Reagan in 1988, Executive Order 12656 designated the National Security Council (NSC) for continuity oversight. This order formalized the federal government's approach to national security emergency preparedness and assigned specific responsibilities to federal departments and agencies.

Presidential Policy Directive 40 (2016)

PPD-40 modernized continuity planning for the contemporary era, with FEMA taking on a leadership role in coordinating and overseeing continuity programs across the federal government. This directive updated the policy framework to address modern threats and operational requirements.

FCD-1: The Core Framework

FCD-1 operates under PPD-40 authority and was developed by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and FEMA. It establishes the "core framework requirements for continuity planning within the federal government, with specific focus on the executive branch."

Under FCD-1, FEMA is authorized to train, exercise, and implement continuity planning across all federal departments and agencies. The directive provides:

  • Principles that guide continuity planning across the executive branch
  • Guidelines for developing comprehensive COOP and COG plans
  • Standards that ensure consistency and interoperability across federal agencies

FCD-2: Implementation Guidance

FCD-2 complements FCD-1 by serving as the practical implementation guidance document. While FCD-1 provides the framework and requirements, FCD-2 delivers "actionable steps and procedures" that agencies use to build and maintain their continuity programs.

FCD-1 and FCD-2 together form the backbone of federal continuity planning. They are professional, civilian-led documents that establish how the government maintains operations during emergencies — through structured, documented, and practiced procedures.
