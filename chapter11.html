CHAPTER 11

The Conspiracy Industry

Follow the money. Follow the audience. Follow the lies.

This May Seem Far-Fetched. Read It Anyway.

What you are about to read may seem unbelievable. A computer technician from New York who has made over 7,700 failed predictions and still has 810,000 followers. A British man living in Spain who got his "Dr." title from a parking ticket and has been promising a global currency reset for six years. A woman whose PhD comes from a school that was shut down by the courts for being "a complete scam," and who claims she attended it two years before it was founded. An insurance investigator from Seattle whose followers believe he is JFK Jr. back from the dead. A former boxer who charges people money to hear predictions that never come true. A man who claims he trained with a fake Navy SEAL who is wanted by U.S. Marshals as a sex offender.

If you listed these people and their claims to any reasonable person, the response would be: nobody would believe this. And yet millions do. Not because the claims are credible, but because the people making them have built a structured industry that functions exactly like any other media business, with creators, distributors, amplifiers, and mainstream figures who give it all an air of legitimacy.

When you see these individuals in isolation, they look like random people on the internet saying outrageous things. But when you see how they are organized, how they cross-promote, how the money flows, and how each tier validates the tier above it, the picture changes. It stops looking random and starts looking like what it is: a business. A business that sells fear and false hope to Americans who are looking for answers in a world that feels broken.

This chapter maps that business from top to bottom. It names the people involved. It shows the connections between them. It explains the revenue model. And it demonstrates why the promises can never be fulfilled, because the moment the "plan" comes true, the revenue stops. Read through the profiles below. By the time you reach the end, the pieces will click together.

The Supply Chain

The conspiracy industry operates on a four-tier supply chain. Each tier serves a specific function, and content flows downward from creators to consumers through a network that mirrors legitimate media distribution.

Tier 1: Content Creators

Jon Herold (Patel Patriot) and Derek Johnson (RattleTrap 1776) produce the original theories. These are the people who write the Substack posts, film the video breakdowns, and construct the narratives that everything else is built on. Their work is covered in detail in Chapters 4 and 5 of this site.

Tier 2: Distribution Networks

We The Media, Anon Alliance, and Badlands Media are the Telegram channels and media networks that push content to hundreds of thousands of subscribers. They function as wire services for the conspiracy world, ensuring that a single person's theory reaches a mass audience within hours.

Tier 3: Amplifiers

X22 Report (Dave Fishman), David "Nino" Rodriguez, Scott McKay, Charlie Ward, Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes, Ariel, InTheMatrixxx (Jeff), Michael Jaco, and Kerry Cassidy are the podcasters and video creators who repackage and amplify the theories to their own audiences. Many add their own "insider intel" claims to the mix.

Tier 4: Mainstream Laundering

Candace Owens, Tucker Carlson, and Megyn Kelly are mainstream media figures who give conspiracy-adjacent ideas credibility by platforming them or echoing their themes to audiences of millions. When a mainstream host repeats a conspiracy framework, it stops looking fringe and starts looking like news.

Mainstream Laundering

X22 Report (David Marc Fishman)

David Marc Fishman is a computer technician from New York who got laid off during the 2008 financial crisis and started "prepping" for the end times. He launched X22 Report in 2013 as a website and podcast predicting imminent economic collapse. Day after day, month after month, year after year, Fishman warned his audience that financial collapse was coming, another war was around the corner, and a false flag attack was about to happen. None of it came true. He also ran a now-defunct website called NewzSentinel, which was flagged as fake news, and troll-tracker Josh Russell tied apparent Iranian trolls to content shared from that site on Reddit.

When Q appeared, Fishman pivoted hard because the audience was bigger and the money was better. He began reading Q drops on his daily livestreams and maintaining a Q drop archive on his website. YouTube banned him for promoting Q content. Spotify banned him. He moved to Rumble, where he now has 810,000 followers and gets between 166,000 and 310,000 views per episode. He publishes two episodes per day, every single day. Episode 3,868 aired on March 26, 2026. That is over 7,700 episodes of predictions that have not come true.

The daily format is the weapon. Fishman conditions his audience through repetition. When someone shows up every single night with the same confident tone and the same message, people start believing it not because the evidence supports it but because they have heard it so many times that it feels true. He told his audience that devolution was real, that the military was in control, that mass arrests were imminent. He read Jon Herold's Substack posts on air and presented them as confirmed intelligence. He warned listeners that three things were always about to happen: a massive cyber attack, a mass shooting event, and then total economic collapse. Year after year, none of it materialized, but the audience stayed because the daily ritual had become a habit.

Fishman's revenue comes from one-time donations, membership subscriptions, and embedded advertising networks across his websites. Media Bias/Fact Check rates X22 Report as a strong conspiracy and pseudoscience website with very low factual reporting. Critics describe him as "one of the biggest conmen associated with the Q conspiracy movement." He is now 3,868 episodes into a show that has never once delivered on its central promise. But the money keeps coming, because the audience is conditioned to show up every night regardless of results.

Part of how Fishman maintains credibility is by getting legitimate people on his show. Devin Nunes and Kash Patel have appeared on X22, not because they endorse Fishman's conspiracy theories, but because they are trying to steer his massive audience toward legitimate sources and accurate information. This is what grifters do. They book real people doing the right thing and use those appearances as validation for everything else on the show. When a former Congressman or a senior Trump official sits across from Dave, the audience does not distinguish between "this person came on to correct the record" and "this person endorses everything Dave says." The guest's credibility transfers to the host, and the host uses it to sell fiction for the next 3,867 episodes.

David "Nino" Rodriguez

Nino Rodriguez is a former professional boxer with no intelligence background, no military experience, no government service, and no journalism training. He charges people money to watch him talk about mass arrests, military tribunals, and secret operations that have never happened and will never happen. His followers literally pay per view to hear predictions that do not come true. When those predictions fail, Nino moves the goalposts and charges for the next stream. The pay-per-view model strips away any pretense of public service. This is not a patriot sharing information. This is a man selling tickets to a show where nothing happens.

Nino cross-promotes constantly with Charlie Ward, Scott McKay, and other Tier 3 figures, creating a closed ecosystem where the same audience cycles between shows, hearing the same failed predictions repackaged by different hosts. Each host validates the others. If Charlie Ward says mass arrests are coming and then Nino says it too, the audience believes it must be true because "multiple sources" are confirming it. In reality, it is the same fiction being repeated by people who all profit from the same lie.

Charlie Ward

Charlie Ward is not even American. He is a British conspiracy theorist living in Spain who built his brand by telling Americans that their government has been secretly overthrown and that a global currency reset will make everyone rich. He calls himself "Dr. Charlie Ward," but according to investigators, he started using the "Dr." title after using it to get out of a parking ticket in the UK. He has used it ever since to make his claims more believable. BBC journalist Shayan Sardarizadeh described Ward as one of the most notorious promoters of the Q conspiracy theory, adding that his views are so extreme that even most Q supporters regard him as a pariah.

Ward promotes NESARA/GESARA and the "Quantum Financial System," a financial fraud scheme that promises a global currency reset, debt forgiveness, and wealth redistribution for everyone. Investigators have called the 25-year NESARA scam "a multi-million dollar fraud empire" and Ward has been specifically called out for "QFS manual sales and fake insider claims." He claims to have been "moving money around the world for private clients ranging from the super-rich to government officials." There is no independent verification of this claim.

Ward's track record of failed predictions is staggering. He and his collaborator Simon Parkes predicted "The Storm" would come on January 6, 2021. When that backfired, it was surely coming on January 20th. When that failed, Trump would be inaugurated on March 4th. Then July 4th. The goalposts never stop moving. The currency reset has been "imminent" for over six years. His own audience has turned on him, with followers publicly asking where the NESARA/GESARA payouts are and commenters calling him "Charlie 'The Grifter' Ward, still singing the exact same tune he has for at least 6 years now."

Ward first rose to YouTube fame by promoting Gregory Hallett as the new King of England, whom he later admitted he knew was a fraud all along. He used the story to grow his channel and then moved on to the next grift. A dedicated Telegram channel exists for exposing Ward and Parkes, claiming both "were well known in the UK for their lies, frauds leaving a string of victims behind them." The BBC conducted a trending investigation titled "Financial Fantasy Ruining Lives" examining the damage caused by these schemes. Ward claims to have over 13 million followers across platforms. If even a fraction of those people have sent him money based on his promises of imminent financial windfalls, the scale of the fraud is enormous.

Investigators have identified specific red flags for these financial conspiracy schemes: any registration fee equals a scam. Secret military operations equal a scam. Specific "chosen" cryptocurrencies equal a scam. Constantly moving dates equal a scam. Leaving regulated exchanges equals a scam. Charlie Ward's content checks every single one of these boxes.

Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes

The "Dr." in Jan Halper-Hayes' name is doing a lot of heavy lifting, and it should not be. Her Ph.D. in Organizational Psychology comes from Columbia Pacific University, an unaccredited distance learning school in California that was shut down by court order in 2000. During the state's 1997 lawsuit to close the school, California Deputy Attorney General Asher Rubin called it "a diploma mill which has been preying on California consumers for too many years" and "a consumer fraud, a complete scam." The Marin County Superior Court ordered it shut down and levied $10,000 in fines for "deceptive and unfair practices."

Columbia Pacific University failed California academic regulations on 88 points. Among the findings: a Ph.D. dissertation written in Spanish was approved by four faculty members who could not speak the language. Another dissertation had "no hypothesis, no data collection, and no statistical analysis" and was characterized as "more like a project paper at the college freshman level." The State of Texas classified CPU as an illegal supplier of educational credentials. The State of Michigan classified it as unacceptable for credentialing. A GAO investigation found federal employees with CPU degrees who completed no coursework. This is where Jan Halper-Hayes got her doctorate.

The timeline problems get worse. Her LinkedIn lists enrollment at Columbia Pacific University from 1976 to 1986. Columbia Pacific University was not founded until 1978. She claims to have attended a school two years before it existed. She also claims to have done "doctoral work under Carl Rogers and Warren Bennis at Fordham University." Carl Rogers spent his final decades at the Center for Studies of the Person in La Jolla, California, not at Fordham University in New York. Rogers died on February 4, 1987. There is no independent verification that Rogers supervised doctoral work at Fordham or that Halper-Hayes studied under him in any formal capacity. Her LinkedIn lists a 2017 "Certificate in Political Psychology" from Stanford University's summer program, which is a short continuing education course, not a degree program. The combination of a diploma mill PhD with impossible enrollment dates, an unverifiable claim about studying under a famous psychologist who was not at the institution she names, and a summer certificate from Stanford creates an impression of academic authority built entirely on sand.

Armed with these credentials, Halper-Hayes went on national television in 2023 and made claims that Trump himself amplified on Truth Social. She claimed that Space Force has evidence proving fraud in the 2020 election. She claimed Trump signaled his intent to dissolve the "U.S. corporation" during a meeting with the Queen of England. She claimed this triggered a transfer of gold from the Vatican Bank. Space Force has never confirmed possessing election fraud evidence. The "U.S. corporation" narrative is a sovereign citizen conspiracy theory debunked in Chapter 8 of this site. No Vatican gold transfer has been documented by any financial institution or government agency.

As of March 25, 2026, Halper-Hayes is still producing content. Before It's News published "Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes: New Q Military 3.25.26, Trump vs the Deep State" two days ago. She has predicted mass arrests, military tribunals, and "the storm" for years. None of it has happened. But a woman with a diploma mill PhD, a vague Fordham connection, and a Stanford summer certificate continues to be platformed as an expert because the "Dr." title bypasses the critical thinking of audiences who would otherwise ask for evidence.

Despite all of this, Halper-Hayes became a regular collaborator with Derek Johnson. She was reportedly one of Derek Johnson's first nine listeners and has since appeared in at least seven joint roundtable discussions with him, often alongside Michael Jaco and SG Anon. These appearances were shared on Before It's News, Rumble, and Bitchute with titles like "Embrace the Suck as Pause Happens for Deep State Takedown" and "Disclosing Never Before Heard Trump Intel." A woman with a diploma mill PhD, impossible enrollment dates, and fabricated academic connections is now a regular on the conspiracy circuit, giving Derek Johnson's stolen theories the appearance of academic credibility. Roseanne Barr has also promoted Derek Johnson and similar conspiracy content, using her celebrity platform to amplify theories she has no expertise to evaluate.

The Ghost published detailed investigations into Halper-Hayes: "From Diploma Mill to 'Doctor'" and "Introducing Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes". A third article compares both figures: "Parallel Paths of Misinformation: Comparing Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes and Derek Johnson".

Think about that for a moment. An anonymous person posts cryptic messages on an image board. Jeff reads those messages, invents an interpretation, and charges people a monthly subscription to hear it. The "decoder" model is dependency by design. Followers cannot understand the "truth" on their own. They need Jeff to translate it. And that translation costs money every single month.

Tier 4: Mainstream Laundering

The most dangerous tier in the conspiracy supply chain is the one that makes fringe ideas look normal. Mainstream laundering occurs when media figures with large, established audiences repeat conspiracy-adjacent frameworks without explicitly endorsing the conspiracy itself. This gives millions of people permission to believe ideas they might otherwise dismiss.

Candace Owens

Candace Owens' trajectory is the radicalization pipeline playing out in real time in front of millions of people. She started as a mainstream conservative commentator at Turning Point USA, the organization founded by Charlie Kirk. She was articulate, effective, and built a massive following. Then she joined The Daily Wire in 2021, where she hosted a popular political talk show. So far, a normal conservative media career.

Then came the shift. Owens began making increasingly antisemitic statements, leading The Daily Wire to part ways with her in March 2024. Ben Shapiro, the company's co-founder, called her "evil" and "twisted." But getting fired did not slow her down. It accelerated her descent. She blamed Israel for the September 11 attacks. She promoted the blood libel, the medieval conspiracy theory that Jews kill Christian children at Passover and use their blood in ceremonial matzah. This is not a fringe claim from an anonymous Telegram account. This is a woman with millions of followers repeating a conspiracy theory that was used to justify pogroms and massacres of Jewish people for over 800 years.

Then on September 10, 2025, Charlie Kirk, the man who gave Owens her first national platform, was assassinated at Utah Valley University. He was 31 years old. He was shot while speaking at an outdoor campus debate. Rather than grieve, Owens turned Kirk's murder into content. She launched a multipart video series called "Bride of Charlie," a serialized attack on Erika Kirk, Charlie's widow, who had stepped in to lead Turning Point USA. The premiere episode received nearly five million views. Owens suggested that the FBI, Israel, and even Turning Point staff could be involved in Kirk's assassination. She obtained leaked internal recordings of Erika Kirk and broadcast them on her show. She said police should be questioning the widow.

This is what the radicalization pipeline produces. A woman who started as a mainstream conservative communicator is now promoting blood libel, blaming Israel for 9/11, and attacking the widow of the man who launched her career, all while millions of people watch. The Jewish advocacy organization AJC has warned that Owens' antisemitic rhetoric "poses real risks for American society." The Forward listed her among the right-wing extremists to watch in 2026. And her audience keeps growing because outrage is the most profitable product in the conspiracy industry.

Tucker Carlson

Tucker Carlson amplified the "Great Replacement" theory on the most-watched cable news show in America. While he did not explicitly endorse Q, his framing of "elites" controlling the country, replacing the population, and suppressing truth mirrored Q narratives closely enough that Q followers cited his segments as validation. When the most-watched cable news host echoes your framework, it stops looking like a conspiracy theory and starts looking like news.

Carlson's role in the supply chain is particularly significant because his audience included millions of people who would never visit a Q-related Telegram channel or watch a Rumble livestream. He brought conspiracy-adjacent framing to dinner tables and living rooms across America, and his viewers had no idea they were consuming repackaged conspiracy content because it came from a man in a suit on a major news network.

Megyn Kelly

Megyn Kelly platformed conspiracy figures and gave them access to mainstream audiences. By treating conspiracy-adjacent claims as legitimate topics for debate, mainstream hosts normalize ideas that would otherwise remain on the fringe. The format of the interview itself does the work. When a conspiracy promoter sits across from a mainstream host, the visual message to the audience is that this person and their ideas are worth taking seriously. The host does not need to agree with the guest. The act of platforming is the endorsement.

The Revenue Model

This is not a volunteer operation. The conspiracy industry generates millions of dollars annually. The people at the top of this supply chain are running businesses, and understanding how the money flows reveals why the content never changes and why the promises are never fulfilled.

The product is fear and false hope. The customers are Americans who feel abandoned by institutions and are looking for answers. And the business model requires that the answers never actually arrive, because the moment the "plan" is fulfilled, the revenue stops. If mass arrests happened tomorrow, there would be no reason to subscribe to the Devolution Power Hour next week. If the military tribunals materialized, there would be no need to buy the next pay-per-view stream. The conspiracy industry is a subscription service for a product that will never be delivered.

Ask yourself this: if these people genuinely believed that a secret military operation was underway to save the country, why would they charge you money to hear about it? If the information were real and the stakes were as high as they claim, sharing it freely would be a patriotic duty. Instead, they put it behind paywalls. That tells you everything you need to know about what they actually believe.

The Real Issues Are Real. The Theories Are Not.

Here is what makes the conspiracy industry so effective and so dangerous: some of what they talk about is true. Sex trafficking is real. Government corruption is real. Institutional failures are real. Epstein was real. These are not conspiracy theories. They are documented facts that demand accountability and action.

The grifters take those real issues and wrap them in garbage. They attach the 3-cannon salute. They attach the two constitutions theory. They attach devolution. They attach military government. They attach NESARA/GESARA. They attach secret military tribunals that have been "imminent" for six years. The real issue is the bait. The conspiracy theory is the hook. And once someone is hooked, the theory becomes more important than the real issue it was attached to.

This is the damage. When someone who cares about sex trafficking starts promoting theories about fake inaugurations and secret military operations, the trafficking issue gets discredited by association. When someone who cares about government accountability starts promoting the idea that a Substack blogger has decoded classified military plans, the accountability movement looks unserious. The conspiracy industry does not help the causes it claims to champion. It exploits them for revenue and discredits them in the process.

That is what makes something a conspiracy theory instead of a conspiracy. A conspiracy is a real event supported by evidence. A conspiracy theory is an unfounded narrative attached to real events to make it seem credible. The people in this chapter take real conspiracies and real problems and use them as packaging for theories that have no evidence, no fulfilled predictions, and no accountability when they turn out to be wrong.

Why It Works

The conspiracy industry exploits real grievances. Americans are right to be frustrated with government corruption, media bias, corporate influence, and institutional failures. Those are real problems that deserve real solutions. But the conspiracy industry does not offer solutions. It offers narratives that feel like solutions while actually making the problems worse.

Every hour spent waiting for "the storm" is an hour not spent on local politics, community organizing, or holding actual officials accountable. Every dollar spent on a pay-per-view conspiracy stream is a dollar not donated to a local campaign or a community organization. Every share of a conspiracy theory is a share that could have been a link to a city council meeting, a school board agenda, or a voter registration drive.

The conspiracy industry does not want you engaged in your community. It wants you engaged with its content. Engaged citizens who attend town halls and vote in local elections are a threat to corrupt officials. Citizens who sit at home watching livestreams about secret military operations are not a threat to anyone. The conspiracy industry pacifies the very people who should be the most active in fixing the problems they are angry about.

The real conspiracy is not a secret military plan to save the country. The real conspiracy is an industry that profits from keeping you angry, afraid, and passive while telling you that someone else is handling it. Nobody is coming to save you. That is not a reason to give up. It is a reason to get involved.
