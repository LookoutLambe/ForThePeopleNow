CHAPTER 11

The Conspiracy Industry

Follow the money. Follow the audience. Follow the lies.

How Conspiracy Became a Business

The conspiracy theory ecosystem is not a disorganized collection of random people sharing ideas. It is a structured industry with a supply chain, revenue models, and distribution networks. Like any industry, it has creators who produce the product, distributors who move it to market, amplifiers who increase demand, and mainstream figures who give it legitimacy. Understanding this structure is the first step to recognizing when you are being sold something disguised as truth.

This chapter maps the conspiracy industry from top to bottom. It names the people involved, explains how the money flows, and shows why the business model depends on the promises never being fulfilled. If the "plan" ever came true, the revenue would stop. The industry needs its audience to keep believing, keep watching, and keep paying.

The Supply Chain

The conspiracy industry operates on a four-tier supply chain. Each tier serves a specific function, and content flows downward from creators to consumers through a network that mirrors legitimate media distribution.

Tier 1: Content Creators

Jon Herold (Patel Patriot) and Derek Johnson (RattleTrap 1776) produce the original theories. These are the people who write the Substack posts, film the video breakdowns, and construct the narratives that everything else is built on. Their work is covered in detail in Chapters 4 and 5 of this site.

Tier 2: Distribution Networks

We The Media, Anon Alliance, and Badlands Media are the Telegram channels and media networks that push content to hundreds of thousands of subscribers. They function as wire services for the conspiracy world, ensuring that a single person's theory reaches a mass audience within hours.

Tier 3: Amplifiers

X22 Report (Dave Fishman), David "Nino" Rodriguez, Scott McKay, Charlie Ward, Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes, Ariel, InTheMatrixxx (Jeff), Michael Jaco, and Kerry Cassidy are the podcasters and video creators who repackage and amplify the theories to their own audiences. Many add their own "insider intel" claims to the mix.

Tier 4: Mainstream Laundering

Candace Owens, Tucker Carlson, and Megyn Kelly are mainstream media figures who give conspiracy-adjacent ideas credibility by platforming them or echoing their themes to audiences of millions. When a mainstream host repeats a conspiracy framework, it stops looking fringe and starts looking like news.

Tier 2: The Distribution Networks

We The Media

We The Media became one of the largest Q-adjacent Telegram channels, aggregating content from dozens of conspiracy creators and pushing it to a massive subscriber base. The channel amplified Jon Herold's devolution series and Derek Johnson's Law of War claims, giving these theories exposure far beyond their original Substack and social media audiences. When Q went silent after the 2020 election, channels like We The Media filled the void by curating content from across the conspiracy industry. They gave followers a reason to keep checking their phones every day, even after Q stopped posting.

Anon Alliance

Anon Alliance was another major aggregation network that cross-posted content across platforms. These networks function like news wire services for the conspiracy world, ensuring that content created by a single person reaches hundreds of thousands of people within hours. A theory posted on Substack at noon can be on 50 Telegram channels by dinner. The speed of distribution is part of what makes the industry so effective. By the time anyone can fact-check a claim, it has already been seen by hundreds of thousands of people.

Badlands Media

Badlands Media was founded by Jon Herold himself. This is where the Devolution Power Hour airs weekly. It is a full media operation with multiple shows, hosts, and a production schedule. What started as a Substack blog became a media company. Badlands is not a side project. It is a business built on the foundation of devolution theory, and it demonstrates how conspiracy content creators can vertically integrate, controlling both the production and distribution of their product.

Tier 3: The Amplifiers

The amplifiers are the engine of the conspiracy industry. They take theories created by Tier 1 and distributed by Tier 2 and repackage them for mass consumption. Each amplifier has their own audience, their own style, and their own revenue streams. Together, they reach millions of Americans every week.

X22 Report (Dave Fishman)

Dave Fishman is a New York computer technician who started the X22 Report in 2013. He was banned from YouTube and Spotify for promoting Q-related content and now operates on Rumble, X, and Truth Social. His livestreams include readings of Q drops, and he maintains a Q drop archive for his audience. Media Bias/Fact Check rates X22 Report as a strong conspiracy and pseudoscience website with very low factual reporting.

Dave pushed devolution theory harder than almost anyone in the amplifier tier. He repeatedly stated that devolution was real to his audience, knowing it drove engagement and revenue. He was one of the primary amplifiers who took Jon Herold's theory from a niche Substack blog to a mass audience. Every time he told his viewers that "the plan is real," he was selling a product. The product was hope, and it came with a price tag.

David "Nino" Rodriguez

David "Nino" Rodriguez is a former professional boxer turned conspiracy content creator. He runs pay-per-view conspiracy content, meaning his followers literally pay money to hear predictions that do not come true. He frequently collaborates with Charlie Ward and other Tier 3 figures, creating a cross-promotion network that keeps audiences circulating within the conspiracy ecosystem.

His content promotes mass arrests, military tribunals, and secret operations that never materialize. The pay-per-view model is the most honest version of the conspiracy business model, because at least it makes the transaction explicit: you are paying money for content. The content just happens to be fiction presented as insider knowledge.

Scott McKay ("Patriot Streetfighter")

Scott McKay is a former bodybuilder and former Los Angeles GOP committee member who built a following under the name "Patriot Streetfighter." He is known for violent rhetoric, including threatening to put "bullets inside" health care providers. When a COVID outbreak occurred at one of his own rallies, he blamed it on a "deep state toxin attack" rather than acknowledging the obvious explanation.

After his father died following a COVID-related stroke, McKay vowed "massive retribution" against shadowy forces rather than acknowledging the virus that killed his father. The FBI considers the sovereign citizen movement that McKay promotes to be a domestic terrorist threat. Despite all of this, he continues producing content in 2026 with guests like Michael Jaco and Kerry Cassidy, and his audience continues to grow.

Charlie Ward

Charlie Ward is a British conspiracy theorist described by BBC journalist Shayan Sardarizadeh as one of the most notorious promoters of the Q conspiracy theory, whose views are so extreme that even most Q supporters regard him as a pariah. He promotes NESARA/GESARA financial fraud, claiming a global currency reset is imminent. He has been making this claim for years. It has never happened.

His followers have publicly asked where the promised financial payouts are, wondering aloud about the "bed meds" and the NESARA/GESARA payouts that were supposed to arrive for everyone. The answer is always the same: it is coming soon, just keep watching, just keep believing, just keep paying for content. The promise of free money is one of the most effective recruitment tools in the conspiracy industry, and Charlie Ward has built his entire brand around it.

Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes

Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes appeared on national television claiming to have insider knowledge about Trump's plans. Her claims gave conspiracy theories an air of academic credibility because of her title, despite offering no verifiable evidence for any of her assertions. The "Dr." in front of her name made audiences more likely to believe what she was saying, which is exactly why she was platformed. Credentials are used as a weapon in the conspiracy industry to bypass the critical thinking of people who would otherwise ask for evidence.

Ariel

Ariel is active on X and is one of the amplifier accounts that pushes conspiracy content to a large following. Accounts like Ariel serve as bridges between the dedicated conspiracy platforms like Telegram and Rumble and the mainstream social media platforms where ordinary Americans spend their time. They bring conspiracy content to audiences who would never seek it out on their own.

InTheMatrixxx (Jeff)

InTheMatrixxx, known as Jeff, is one of the most prominent Q "decoders" who built a following by interpreting Q drops and connecting them to current events. He monetized his audience through Patreon and merchandise, building a business around the act of reading anonymous 8chan posts and telling people what they supposedly meant. The "decoder" model is particularly effective because it creates dependency. Followers cannot understand the "truth" on their own. They need Jeff to explain it to them, and that explanation costs money.

Michael Jaco

Michael Jaco is a former Navy SEAL who leverages his military background to give conspiracy content an air of authority. Like Dr. Jan Halper-Hayes, his credentials serve as a tool to bypass audience skepticism. When a former Navy SEAL tells you that secret military operations are happening behind the scenes, it carries more weight than when an anonymous account says the same thing. Jaco is a regular guest on Patriot Streetfighter and other Tier 3 shows.

Kerry Cassidy

Kerry Cassidy runs Project Camelot, a long-running conspiracy media operation that predates the Q movement. She covers topics ranging from secret space programs to alien disclosure to government cover-ups. Her integration into the Q and devolution ecosystem demonstrates how the conspiracy industry absorbs existing audiences. People who followed Kerry Cassidy for UFO content were introduced to devolution theory and Q through cross-promotion with other Tier 3 amplifiers.

Tier 4: Mainstream Laundering

The most dangerous tier in the conspiracy supply chain is the one that makes fringe ideas look normal. Mainstream laundering occurs when media figures with large, established audiences repeat conspiracy-adjacent frameworks without explicitly endorsing the conspiracy itself. This gives millions of people permission to believe ideas they might otherwise dismiss.

Candace Owens

Candace Owens lost her position at The Daily Wire after making increasingly antisemitic statements. Her departure demonstrated that there is a line even in conservative media, but by the time she crossed it she had already amplified conspiracy-adjacent narratives to millions of followers. The damage to her audience's information environment was already done. Once a person has been told that shadowy forces control the media and the government, it does not matter if the person who told them later gets fired for going too far. The seed has been planted.

Tucker Carlson

Tucker Carlson amplified the "Great Replacement" theory on the most-watched cable news show in America. While he did not explicitly endorse Q, his framing of "elites" controlling the country, replacing the population, and suppressing truth mirrored Q narratives closely enough that Q followers cited his segments as validation. When the most-watched cable news host echoes your framework, it stops looking like a conspiracy theory and starts looking like news.

Carlson's role in the supply chain is particularly significant because his audience included millions of people who would never visit a Q-related Telegram channel or watch a Rumble livestream. He brought conspiracy-adjacent framing to dinner tables and living rooms across America, and his viewers had no idea they were consuming repackaged conspiracy content because it came from a man in a suit on a major news network.

Megyn Kelly

Megyn Kelly platformed conspiracy figures and gave them access to mainstream audiences. By treating conspiracy-adjacent claims as legitimate topics for debate, mainstream hosts normalize ideas that would otherwise remain on the fringe. The format of the interview itself does the work. When a conspiracy promoter sits across from a mainstream host, the visual message to the audience is that this person and their ideas are worth taking seriously. The host does not need to agree with the guest. The act of platforming is the endorsement.

The Revenue Model

This is not a volunteer operation. The conspiracy industry generates millions of dollars annually. The people at the top of this supply chain are running businesses, and understanding how the money flows reveals why the content never changes and why the promises are never fulfilled.

Substack
Paid subscriptions (Jon Herold's devolution series)
Amazon
Book sales (Derek Johnson's "Midnight Rider")
Pay-Per-View
Premium conspiracy content (Nino Rodriguez)
Donations
"Patriot" merchandise and direct contributions
Speaking Fees
Events like the ReAwaken America Tour
Rumble
Monetization and ad revenue on alternative platforms
Telegram
Premium channels with exclusive "intel"

The product is fear and false hope. The customers are Americans who feel abandoned by institutions and are looking for answers. And the business model requires that the answers never actually arrive, because the moment the "plan" is fulfilled, the revenue stops. If mass arrests happened tomorrow, there would be no reason to subscribe to the Devolution Power Hour next week. If the military tribunals materialized, there would be no need to buy the next pay-per-view stream. The conspiracy industry is a subscription service for a product that will never be delivered.

Ask yourself this: if these people genuinely believed that a secret military operation was underway to save the country, why would they charge you money to hear about it? If the information were real and the stakes were as high as they claim, sharing it freely would be a patriotic duty. Instead, they put it behind paywalls. That tells you everything you need to know about what they actually believe.

Why It Works

The conspiracy industry exploits real grievances. Americans are right to be frustrated with government corruption, media bias, corporate influence, and institutional failures. Those are real problems that deserve real solutions. But the conspiracy industry does not offer solutions. It offers narratives that feel like solutions while actually making the problems worse.

Every hour spent waiting for "the storm" is an hour not spent on local politics, community organizing, or holding actual officials accountable. Every dollar spent on a pay-per-view conspiracy stream is a dollar not donated to a local campaign or a community organization. Every share of a conspiracy theory is a share that could have been a link to a city council meeting, a school board agenda, or a voter registration drive.

The conspiracy industry does not want you engaged in your community. It wants you engaged with its content. Engaged citizens who attend town halls and vote in local elections are a threat to corrupt officials. Citizens who sit at home watching livestreams about secret military operations are not a threat to anyone. The conspiracy industry pacifies the very people who should be the most active in fixing the problems they are angry about.

The real conspiracy is not a secret military plan to save the country. The real conspiracy is an industry that profits from keeping you angry, afraid, and passive while telling you that someone else is handling it. Nobody is coming to save you. That is not a reason to give up. It is a reason to get involved.
