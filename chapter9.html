CHAPTER 9

Q and the Anon Movement

The anonymous posts that built an industry of misinformation
Part 1: What It Is

What is Q?

On October 28, 2017, an anonymous post appeared on 4chan — a largely unmoderated imageboard known for trolling, offensive content, and complete anonymity. The poster claimed to be a senior U.S. government official with "Q clearance" and insider knowledge of a secret war being waged behind the scenes. The post made a specific, testable prediction: Hillary Clinton would be arrested between 7:45 AM and 8:30 AM on Monday, October 30, 2017.

She wasn't.

But that didn't stop what came next. Over the following five years, this anonymous poster — known simply as "Q" — would publish 4,966 cryptic messages across 4chan, 8chan, and eventually 8kun. These posts blended real, verifiable information about government corruption with fabricated narratives about secret military operations, mass arrests, and a coming "storm" that would expose a global criminal cabal. The real information made the fiction credible. The fiction kept people coming back.

What started as anonymous posts on an obscure imageboard grew into a movement that spanned every major social media platform, spawned dozens of derivative theories, destroyed families, inspired violence, and generated millions of dollars for the content creators who monetized it. It became the foundation for the devolution theory (Chapter 4), Derek Johnson's law of war claims (Chapter 5), and recycled the sovereign citizen movement's 1871 conspiracy (Chapter 8).

This chapter examines Q from the ground up: what it actually was, how it worked, who was behind it, why people believed it, and the real damage it caused.

Q and the Anons — They Are Not the Same Thing

There is no "QAnon." That's a media-created label. There is Q — the anonymous poster who dropped cryptic messages on image boards. And there are the Anons — the followers who tried to decode those messages. They are two separate things. Merging them into "QAnon" was a media convenience that obscured who was actually responsible for what. Q posted. The Anons followed. The grifters monetized. Understanding this distinction matters because it shows how the ecosystem actually worked.

The Anons were mostly regular people who were genuinely concerned about their country and looking for answers. Many were sincere patriots who got pulled into a narrative that exploited their patriotism. The real villains aren't the Anons — they're the grifters who knew better and profited anyway.

Let's strip away the mythology and look at what Q actually was:

  • Anonymous posts on an imageboard. Q posted on 4chan, then 8chan (renamed 8kun) — websites known for anonymity, trolling, and offensive content. Anyone can post anonymously on these boards.
  • Cryptic questions, not answers. Q posts are mostly rhetorical questions designed to lead readers to predetermined conclusions. "What if X? Who controls Y? Think about Z." This technique makes the reader feel like they figured it out, when they were actually led there.
  • No verified identity. Despite claiming "Q clearance" (a Department of Energy security clearance, not a military one), Q has never proven their identity. The "Q clearance" claim itself reveals a misunderstanding — DOE Q clearance has nothing to do with military intelligence.
  • Truth mixed with fiction. Here's what makes Q effective and dangerous: many Q drops contained real, verifiable information. Real corruption. Real issues with government overreach. Real problems with media bias. But those truths were deliberately woven together with false narratives — secret military operations, imminent mass arrests, martial law, "the storm." By mixing real truths with fiction, the entire package felt credible. This is the classic malinformation technique: use real facts to make the lies believable. The major predictions — Hillary's arrest, mass tribunals, "the storm" — never materialized.
This is the most important thing to understand about Q: the truths were the bait, and the conspiracy narrative was the hook. When someone showed you a Q drop about real government corruption and it checked out, you were more likely to believe the next drop about secret military operations — even though that one was fiction. This is how propaganda has always worked.

Actual Q Drops: Read Them Yourself

Don't take our word for it. Here are actual Q posts so you can see what you were being asked to believe:

Q Drop #1 (October 28, 2017)

"HRC extradition already in motion... Expect massive riots organized in defiance and others fleeing the US... Hillary Clinton will be arrested between 7:45 AM - 8:30 AM EST on Monday - the morning of Oct 30, 2017."

What happened: Hillary Clinton was not arrested on October 30, 2017. She has never been arrested. This was Q's very first prediction. It was wrong on day one.

Q Drop #34 (November 1, 2017)

"On POTUS' order, we have initiated certain fail-safes... upon the arrest announcement of Mr. Podesta (actionable 11.4). On POTUS' order, a state of temporary military control will be actioned and special ops carried out... We will be initiating the Emergency Broadcast System during this time..."

What happened: Podesta was not arrested. No military control was enacted. The Emergency Broadcast System was not activated. Special operations were not carried out against domestic targets. Every single claim in this post was false.

Q Drop #4966 (November 27, 2022) — The Last Drop

"What is coded in your DNA? Who put it there? Why? Mankind is repressed. We will be repressed no more... There is a war for your DNA. Protect your DNA. Ascension."

What happened: Q's final post abandoned any pretense of insider intelligence and devolved into pseudo-spiritual mysticism about DNA and "ascension." From predicting arrests and military operations to... DNA warfare and spiritual ascension. That's where five years of "trust the plan" ended up.

Q's first post predicted Hillary's arrest. Q's last post was about DNA ascension. In between: 4,964 posts, zero mass arrests, zero military tribunals, zero Emergency Broadcast activations, zero "storms." But millions of dollars generated for the content creators who monetized the narrative.

The Social Control: Shill vs. Patriot

And here's the mechanism that kept people trapped: if you questioned Q, you were a shill and a bot. If you agreed with it, you were a patriot.

This is textbook cult behavior — not patriotism. In a healthy community, questioning is encouraged. In a cult, questioning is punished. The Q community created a binary: you're either "awake" (inside the group) or you're "asleep" (an enemy). There was no middle ground. No room for "I agree with some of this but I'm not sure about that part."

The moment you asked "but where's the evidence?" you were labeled a shill, a fed, a bot, or controlled opposition. The moment you agreed without questioning, you were welcomed as a "digital soldier" and a "patriot." This social pressure kept people repeating claims they hadn't verified because the cost of questioning was losing your community — and the reward for conformity was belonging.

Cult Behavior: Question the narrative = you're attacked, labeled a shill/bot, and excluded from the group. Conform to the narrative = you're praised as a patriot and welcomed into the community.
Healthy Research: In legitimate research, questioning is the entire point. Peer review exists specifically to challenge claims. Scientists, lawyers, emergency managers — all professionals WELCOME scrutiny of their work. If your "truth movement" can't survive a question, it's not a truth movement. It's a cult.

The Book of Q: When Conspiracy Becomes Religion

Perhaps the most disturbing development in the Q movement is its transformation into a pseudo-religion — complete with its own scripture, prophecy, and messiah figure.

Followers created a "Book of Q" — a compilation of Q drops formatted like a Bible. But here's the deeper irony: there is already a "Lost Gospel of Q" in Christian theology. The Q Source (from German "Quelle" meaning "source") is a hypothetical collection of Jesus Christ's original sayings that scholars believe was used as source material for the Gospels of Matthew and Luke. It's a serious academic concept in biblical scholarship.

The Q movement — whether intentionally or not — co-opted the very name of a document believed to contain the actual words of Christ and replaced it with anonymous imageboard posts. Followers then treated those posts with the same reverence as scripture. Think about that: they took the name of Christ's own words and gave it to an anonymous internet poster.

Academic researchers from Eastern Illinois University documented how Q neither corrected nor scolded followers who compared Q and Trump to Christ. By tacitly approving these comparisons, Q solidified the construction of Trump as a "publicly misunderstood Messiah." In one instance, Q equated himself, Trump, and the biblical God as equals in the battle against evil.

Let that sink in. An anonymous poster on an imageboard — likely a website administrator named Ron Watkins — allowed his followers to equate him with Jesus Christ. And they did. The Q movement didn't just become a political movement. It became a religion where questioning the narrative is treated as heresy, not disagreement.

Scholar Marc-André Argentino has classified the Q movement as a "new religious movement" — a "hyper-real religion" that evolved from conspiracy theory into something people organize their lives around. Online communities now interpret passages of the Bible through the lens of Q drops. That's not faith — that's replacing scripture with anonymous internet posts.

For Christians who follow Q: consider what you're actually following. An anonymous internet poster who allowed himself to be compared to Christ. Who predicted events that never happened. Who led people away from their churches and families. Who turned the Bible into a decoder ring for political conspiracy theories. That is not Christianity. That is idolatry.

How the Q Technique Works

Q's method is psychologically effective because it exploits several cognitive biases:

Leading Questions

Instead of making falsifiable claims, Q asks questions: "What if the military is in control?" This lets followers fill in the blanks with their own assumptions, making them feel like investigators rather than consumers.

Vague "Predictions"

Posts are deliberately vague so they can be retroactively matched to any event. "Something big is coming" can be applied to anything that happens later. Followers call these "Q proofs."

Insider Language

Abbreviations, code words, and references to classified programs create an in-group feeling. If you understand the language, you feel special — part of an elite group that "sees the truth."

"Trust the Plan"

When predictions fail (which they always do), the response is always "trust the plan" — things are happening behind the scenes that you can't see. This makes the theory unfalsifiable. No evidence can disprove it because absence of evidence is reframed as evidence of secrecy.

Part 3: How It Spread

The Platform Spread: From 4chan to Your Living Room

Q started on 4chan — a dark corner of the internet most normal people had never heard of. But it didn't stay there. It spread like a virus across every major platform:

Facebook, Twitter, TruthSocial, X, 8kun, Telegram, WhatsApp, Rumble, Gab, Parler — they all played this Q hand like it was a massive "patriotic" backchannel to combating corruption in the United States.

And here's the irony: the mainstream media helped spread it. Every negative story about Q made people curious. They'd search it up. And instead of doing their homework and checking sources, they ate it like candy. The media's negative coverage acted as free advertising for the movement.

The Bezmenov Warning

In 1984, former KGB agent Yuri Bezmenov gave an interview describing how the Soviet Union used ideological subversion to destabilize nations. He described a process of spoonfeeding disinformation little by little until people accepted it as reality. The four stages he described were:

  1. Demoralization — Destroy faith in institutions (government, media, education, military)
  2. Destabilization — Create chaos in the economy, foreign relations, and defense
  3. Crisis — A period of upheaval where people demand change at any cost
  4. Normalization — The new reality becomes accepted; the population stops resisting

And remember what Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev reportedly said:

"We will take America without firing a shot. We do not have to invade the United States, we will destroy you from within."

Sound familiar? The Q movement and its derivatives accomplished stages 1 and 2 more effectively than the KGB ever could have imagined — and Americans did it to themselves. The movement destroyed trust in every institution: the courts, the military chain of command, the election system, the media, the government. That's not patriotism. That's exactly what Khrushchev predicted and Bezmenov warned about.

The WW2 Radio Parallel

This isn't new. Q used the exact same technique Nazi Germany perfected during World War II with radio propaganda. Joseph Goebbels understood that if you control the information channel and repeat a narrative with enough authority, people accept it as truth. The Nazi regime broadcast cryptic, authoritative messages that listeners decoded and spread through their communities.

Q drops functioned identically: cryptic messages from an unseen "authority" that followers had to decode and share. The medium changed from radio to imageboards and Telegram, but the technique is the same — create a sense of insider knowledge, repeat the narrative relentlessly, and let the audience do the spreading for you. As Goebbels himself said: repeat a lie often enough and it becomes accepted as truth.

The fact that American patriots — people who would have fought against this exact type of propaganda in 1944 — fell for it in 2020 should tell you everything about how effective these techniques are when wrapped in the flag.

Foreign adversaries didn't need to run Q. They just needed Americans to distrust their own institutions — and the Q movement delivered that on a silver platter. Whether Q was a foreign operation, a domestic grift, or a website admin's power trip, the result was the same: millions of Americans now distrust the very systems that protect their freedom.

Part 4: The Reality

Who is Actually Behind Q?

Multiple investigations, including the HBO documentary "Q: Into the Storm," have traced Q's identity to Ron Watkins and his father Jim Watkins, who own and operate 8kun (formerly 8chan) — the only platform where Q posted.

Ron Watkins was the administrator of 8kun. He controlled the board where Q posted. He had the technical ability to post as Q or verify Q's identity. During the HBO documentary, he appeared to accidentally admit involvement before catching himself.

Think about what that means: the "high-level government insider" with "Q clearance" was likely the website administrator of an anonymous imageboard. Not a military intelligence officer. Not someone with access to classified operations. A website admin.

The Real-World Harm

The Q movement isn't just wrong — it has caused real damage to real people:

  • Families destroyed. Thousands of families have been torn apart by the Q movement. Parents who fell down the rabbit hole became unrecognizable to their children. Marriages ended. Relationships severed. The subreddit r/QAnonCasualties documented hundreds of these stories.
  • Violence. Q-related beliefs have been linked to multiple violent incidents, including the January 6 Capitol breach where Q symbols and slogans were prominent.
  • Financial exploitation. Followers have spent thousands on merchandise, paid subscriptions, conference tickets, and donations to influencers who profit from the narrative.
  • Civic disengagement. "Trust the plan" turned active citizens into passive consumers waiting for a secret military operation that will never come — instead of participating in the actual democratic process.
