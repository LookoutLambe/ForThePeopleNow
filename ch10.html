CHAPTER 10

FEMA Camps

The theory that keeps Americans from getting help when they need it most

The Claim

The federal government has built a network of secret concentration camps across the United States. FEMA is running them. When martial law is declared — and it's coming — they will round up patriots, gun owners, preppers, conservatives, and dissidents and send them to these camps. The camps are already built. The railroad cars are already equipped. The mass graves are already dug. All they need is the order.

That is the FEMA camps theory, and it has been circulating in one form or another since the early 1990s — passed from militias and survivalist networks to shortwave radio programs, then to YouTube channels and Telegram accounts with hundreds of thousands of followers. It has never been proven, and it has been debunked repeatedly with sourced documentation every time it surfaces. And yet it keeps coming back, because it is useful — not to the people who believe it, but to the people who sell it.

Where It Started: Rex 84 and the Militia Era

This theory did not come from nowhere. Like most durable conspiracy theories, it has a kernel of something real at its center — real enough to sound credible to someone who hasn't read the actual documents.

REX 84: What It Actually Was

In 1984, the Reagan administration conducted a classified exercise called Readiness Exercise 1984 (REX 84), written by Lieutenant Colonel Oliver North, who served as both the NSC White House Aide and the NSC liaison to FEMA. The exercise explored what the government would do in the event of mass civil unrest or widespread domestic opposition to a U.S. military invasion of Central America. The scenarios it examined included declaring martial law, placing military commanders in charge of state and local governments, and detaining large numbers of people deemed threats to national security. It was a planning document for extreme scenarios — not an operational order that was ever executed.

REX 84 was exposed during the Iran-Contra hearings in 1987 when Congressman Jack Brooks asked Oliver North directly whether he had worked on a contingency plan to suspend the American Constitution. Senator Daniel Inouye, the committee chair, cut the question off immediately, ruling it classified. The moment is on video, preserved in the C-SPAN archive, and it is genuinely striking — a sitting congressman attempts to expose a classified government contingency plan on live television, and is silenced by his own party's chairman in real time.

Col. North questioned on REX 84 — Iran-Contra Hearings, July 13, 1987 · C-SPAN

The Claim: REX 84 proves the government has a standing, active plan to suspend the Constitution and imprison Americans in FEMA concentration camps right now.
What REX 84 Was and Wasn't

REX 84 was real, and it was genuinely alarming as a planning document — Oliver North wrote contingency provisions for martial law and mass detention into a government exercise, and it was classified for years. That is a legitimate concern about executive power overreach, and it deserved the scrutiny Jack Brooks tried to give it. The problem is what conspiracy media did with it afterward: they took a 1984 planning exercise that was never activated, never funded for implementation, and never built into operational infrastructure, and turned it into proof that FEMA is operating concentration camps today.

Planning for a worst-case scenario is not the same as executing it. Every emergency management exercise examines scenarios that would be catastrophic if they occurred — that is the point of the exercise. REX 84's scenarios never materialized. No martial law was declared. No mass detentions were carried out. No camps were built. The planning document existed; the camps did not. Those are two entirely different things, and collapsing them is exactly how this theory survives.

The 1990s Militia Movement

REX 84 was the seed. The 1990s militia movement watered it. After Ruby Ridge (1992), Waco (1993), and the Oklahoma City bombing (1995), anti-government sentiment in militia circles was at its peak. Shortwave radio programs like The Patriot Report and publications like The Militia of Montana Newsletter circulated the FEMA camps theory widely. By the late 1990s it was standard militia doctrine: FEMA was the enforcement arm of the coming New World Order, and the camps were ready.

None of it was true, and none of it was checked — the internet was still young, and alternative media was a network of fax machines and photocopied newsletters circulating among people who already believed. A theory that spread through those channels in 1995 arrived on YouTube in 2008 with the same claims, freshly packaged with new "evidence," and an entire generation of people who had never heard it encountered it as if it were breaking news.

The "Evidence": What They're Actually Showing You

Every version of the FEMA camps theory comes packaged with evidence — photos, videos, documents, and eyewitness accounts, all presented with the confidence of someone who has never spoken to an emergency manager, a railroad worker, or a city zoning official. Let's go through the most common pieces and explain what they actually are.

H.R. 645 — The "FEMA Camp Bill"

The Claim: H.R. 645, the National Emergency Centers Establishment Act, introduced in 2009, proves Congress was preparing to build FEMA detention camps on military bases.
What H.R. 645 Actually Said

H.R. 645 was a bill introduced by Rep. Alcee Hastings (D-FL) that proposed using closed or underutilized military installations as emergency relief centers — places to house disaster victims, coordinate aid distribution, and provide temporary shelter during major disasters. Think post-Katrina, where thousands of displaced Americans needed shelter for weeks. The bill proposed building that capacity in advance rather than scrambling after each disaster. It was not a detention bill. It did not authorize incarcerating anyone. It never passed. It died in committee. The "FEMA camp bill" was an emergency relief infrastructure proposal that failed to become law, and was turned by conspiracy media into proof of a secret detention network.

The Railroad Cars

The Claim: Photographs circulate showing railroad cars outfitted with "shackles" and "ankle restraints" — proof that the government is pre-positioning mass transportation for roundups.
What Those Railroad Cars Actually Are

The photographs most often cited show autorack cars — multi-level railroad cars used to transport automobiles and trucks. The "shackles" are vehicle tie-down straps and anchor points used to secure cars during transport so they don't roll around. Railroad hobbyists, train photographers, and automotive shipping professionals have explained this repeatedly. The anchor points are standardized, visible, and documented in railroad industry publications. They are not restraints for human beings. They are how Ford F-150s get shipped from Michigan to dealerships in Arizona. The people promoting this claim either didn't ask anyone in the railroad industry, or asked and didn't care what the answer was.

The "Empty" FEMA Facilities

The Claim: Aerial photos and satellite images show large, fenced compounds sitting empty around the country — pre-built FEMA detention facilities waiting for the order to fill them.
What Those Facilities Actually Are

They are industrial facilities, water treatment plants, power substations, military training areas, National Guard armories, and prison complexes — all of which are large, fenced, and sometimes photographed from above in ways that look ominous if you want them to. Emergency management staging areas also exist: pre-positioned equipment and supply depots maintained by FEMA for rapid deployment after disasters. These are publicly documented, described in FEMA's National Response Framework, and serve the exact function their name describes — storing disaster relief supplies so they can be deployed within hours of a major event. The "empty camps" are either normal infrastructure misidentified by people who haven't done basic research, or legitimate emergency staging facilities that are supposed to be there.

The Walmart Stores

In 2015, five Walmart stores in Texas closed simultaneously for "plumbing repairs." Within 48 hours, the internet had decided this was the beginning of Operation Jade Helm 15 — a military exercise in the southwest — and that the Walmarts were being converted to FEMA processing centers connected by tunnels. Texas Governor Greg Abbott activated the Texas State Guard to "monitor" the federal military exercise. The stores reopened after plumbing repairs were completed. No tunnels were found. No one was processed. The state guard found a Walmart doing plumbing work.

Operation Jade Helm 15 was a routine special operations training exercise — soldiers trained in civilian terrain across the southwest and then went home, exactly as the exercise plan described. No martial law was declared, no one was arrested, and the "FEMA Walmart processing centers" turned out to be stores getting their plumbing fixed. This is what happens when a 30-year-old conspiracy theory collides with an ordinary news event: everything becomes evidence, nothing can disprove anything, and a governor mobilizes the state guard to monitor a plumber.

Who Promotes It and Why

The FEMA camps theory didn't survive 30 years by accident. It survived because it is profitable, and because the people promoting it face no consequences for being wrong.

Alex Jones

No one has done more to spread the FEMA camps theory than Alex Jones. He has been warning about FEMA detention camps for decades on Infowars, on his radio program, and in documentaries like Police State (1999) and The Obama Deception (2009). The camps are "ready." The order is "coming." The "globalists" are "moving fast." Buy the water filters. Stock the freeze-dried food. Subscribe to the newsletter.

Alex Jones was found liable for defamation in the Sandy Hook cases and ordered to pay nearly $1.5 billion in damages for spreading lies about that massacre. His business model — sell fear, sell products to people afraid of what you're telling them — is documented, successful, and ongoing. The FEMA camps theory is a cornerstone of that model because it can never be definitively disproven. The camps are always "coming." The order is always "imminent." There is always a reason to stay subscribed.

Jesse Ventura and Conspiracy Theory

In 2012, truTV's Conspiracy Theory with Jesse Ventura ran an episode dedicated to FEMA camps. Ventura visited locations described as camps and filmed ominous-looking facilities. The episode was pulled from rotation after its first airing — which, naturally, became its own evidence of a coverup. What the episode actually showed was a mix of industrial facilities, legitimate emergency management infrastructure, and creative camera angles. The "investigation" did not involve talking to emergency managers, reviewing FEMA's publicly available planning documents, or interviewing anyone with actual expertise in continuity or disaster preparedness. It involved dramatic music and suspicious stares at chain-link fences.

The Telegram and Podcast Ecosystem

The theory has fully migrated to modern platforms. Telegram channels with hundreds of thousands of subscribers post satellite images of "new FEMA facilities" regularly. YouTube channels run hours of "documentation." Podcasts sell emergency preparedness bundles alongside the warnings. The structure is always the same: create fear, establish urgency, sell the product that addresses it. The FEMA camps theory is an excellent marketing tool because it simultaneously creates the anxiety and identifies the solution — buy this, prep that, subscribe here, trust us not them.

What FEMA Actually Does

The Federal Emergency Management Agency was established in 1979 under President Carter with a straightforward mission: help Americans before, during, and after disasters. In practice that means coordinating federal response to presidentially declared emergencies, managing the National Flood Insurance Program, funding state and local emergency management agencies, pre-positioning food and water ahead of hurricanes, running Individual Assistance programs for displaced families, and training the emergency managers who show up when everything goes wrong.

FEMA has real, documented failures — Hurricane Katrina (2005) was a catastrophic breakdown of federal emergency response at every level, and the agency has faced legitimate criticism for slow response times, bureaucratic dysfunction, and inequitable distribution of aid in disasters since. Those criticisms are worth making precisely because they are grounded in fact and lead to accountability, reform, and better outcomes. They are a completely different category of complaint from "FEMA is building concentration camps to imprison gun owners."

What FEMA Actually Does

  • Coordinates federal disaster declarations
  • Pre-positions food, water, and supplies before disasters
  • Opens disaster relief centers for survivors
  • Provides Individual Assistance grants to displaced families
  • Trains state and local emergency managers
  • Manages the National Flood Insurance Program
  • Runs continuity of government planning frameworks
  • Coordinates with the Red Cross and NGOs

What the Theory Claims FEMA Does

  • Operates secret concentration camps
  • Pre-positions railroad cars with shackles
  • Coordinates with the military for mass roundups
  • Waits for the order to suspend the Constitution
  • Converts Walmart stores into processing centers
  • Digs mass graves in advance
  • Targets gun owners, patriots, and preppers
  • Serves the New World Order / globalist agenda

One of these lists is documented in FEMA's publicly available planning documents, congressional testimony, and 40+ years of operational history. The other is documented in Telegram posts, YouTube videos, and Alex Jones merchandise catalogs.

The Real Cost: When People Believe It

This is not an academic debate about the finer points of emergency management theory — people believe this, and when they do, they make decisions based on it during the worst moments of their lives.

During Hurricane Katrina, thousands of people in New Orleans and the surrounding parishes refused to go to government-run shelters. Some refused because the shelters were genuinely inadequate, which was a legitimate criticism that warranted a legitimate response. But others refused because they had been told, repeatedly, that government shelters were the first step toward detention — and so they stayed in their homes while the water rose around them.

During COVID-19, the FEMA camps theory found more fuel than it had seen in decades — and some of that fuel was real.

Governors made decisions that killed people. That is not hyperbole. Governor Cuomo's March 25, 2020 order forced New York nursing homes to accept COVID-positive patients and explicitly prohibited them from requiring a negative test before readmission. His administration then altered the official DOH report — cutting the reported death count from over 9,000 to 6,400 by excluding hospital deaths — and withheld the real data from federal investigators. His aide Melissa DeRosa admitted on a recorded call that they froze the data release specifically because they were afraid it would be used against them. The New York State Comptroller eventually documented 13,147 nursing home deaths. The House Select Subcommittee on the Coronavirus Pandemic referred Cuomo for criminal prosecution in October 2024. Governor Whitmer ran a nearly identical policy in Michigan. The state auditor found Michigan had undercounted nursing home deaths by 30 to 42 percent. These were not FEMA operations. These were governors making decisions that the data later confirmed cost lives, and then suppressing that data.

Hospitals had a financial incentive structure built into the CARES Act — Section 3710 — that added a 20% bonus to Medicare reimbursements for any patient diagnosed with COVID-19. Ventilated patients triggered approximately $39,000 in payments. Dr. Scott Jensen, a Minnesota state senator and family physician, raised this publicly and was investigated by the state medical board, which dropped the complaint without action. The CDC's own Vaccine Safety Datalink caught the myocarditis signal in young males from mRNA vaccines before the public-facing VAERS system reflected it — meaning the internal data was ahead of what was being communicated outward. VAERS itself was structurally underequipped: a Harvard Pilgrim Health Care study commissioned by HHS found fewer than 1% of adverse events were ever reported to it. The adverse effects existed. The tracking existed. The public communication did not match either.

And then there was what people could actually see with their own eyes. FEMA and the National Guard deployed Points of Distribution — PODs — across the country for testing and vaccination. These were large-scale, government-run, uniformed operations set up in parking lots, fairgrounds, and public spaces. For anyone already primed by 30 years of FEMA camps content, it looked like confirmation. Tents. Fencing. Military vehicles. Uniformed personnel checking people in and directing them through lines. The theory did not create that image out of nothing — it grafted its existing framework onto a real visible operation and told people what they were seeing was a rehearsal for something worse.

Here is where accuracy requires a distinction. The grievances were real. The nursing home deaths were real. The financial incentive structure was real. The vaccine adverse effects were real and underreported. The PODs were real. None of that is disputed. What the FEMA camps theory added on top of all of it — that the PODs were pre-positioned detention infrastructure, that the hospitals were part of a deliberate population control operation, that the National Guard was practicing for martial law — that part has no evidence. The theory took legitimate failures of government leadership and converted them into a unified conspiracy that pointed every bad thing in one coordinated direction. That conversion is the lie. Not the underlying failures, which deserved and still deserve accountability.

During wildfires in the western United States, some evacuees refused official evacuation shelters and staging areas — again citing FEMA camps — and sheltered in place in the path of active fires rather than accept help from the government they had been taught to fear.

The theory has a body count. Not from FEMA camps — those don't exist. From real disasters where real Americans refused legitimate help because they believed the people trying to save them were actually coming to imprison them. That is what 30 years of this theory has accomplished. It has made Americans afraid of their own disaster relief infrastructure. And the people who spread it have never been held accountable for a single death.

Emergency managers — the professionals who run county emergency management offices, who coordinate shelter operations, who work 72-hour shifts during disasters — are not agents of a secret government detention network. They are, overwhelmingly, local government employees and volunteers. They are your neighbors. They are veterans, firefighters, nurses, and logistics professionals who spent years getting certified so they could help their communities survive disasters. The FEMA camps theory tells Americans those people are the enemy. It is one of the most directly harmful conspiracy theories in circulation because it targets the exact moment when Americans are most vulnerable and most in need of help.

Unfalsifiable by Design

Here is why the FEMA camps theory never dies: it is designed to survive any evidence against it.

If no camps are found: they're hidden. If a facility is identified and turns out to be a water treatment plant: that's what they want you to think. If a government official denies it: they're lying. If a journalist investigates and finds nothing: they're in on it. If someone with emergency management credentials explains the actual purpose of FEMA's facilities: they're a shill or a fed.

This is not a theory that can be tested. It is a belief system. And like all belief systems built on unfalsifiability, it serves its promoters perfectly — because they can never be proven wrong. The camps are always "coming." The evidence is always "mounting." The reveal is always "soon."

The same structure that protects the FEMA camps theory from disproof also protects the devolution theory, the law of war theory, and every other theory examined in this book. When evidence against the claim becomes evidence for the claim, you are no longer dealing with a theory. You are dealing with a revenue model dressed as a worldview.

The Bottom Line

Every single piece of evidence for this theory has a documented, sourced, publicly available explanation. Every single one. And yet the theory continues because the people promoting it are not interested in evidence — they are interested in your attention and your money. As long as you are afraid of FEMA, you will not go to a FEMA shelter. As long as you avoid FEMA shelters, you need to buy survival products from someone else. That someone else is, very often, the exact same person who told you to be afraid of FEMA in the first place.

When a disaster hits — and in America, disasters hit — the people at that shelter are not your enemies. They are local government employees and volunteers who trained for years so they could be useful when everything goes wrong. The theory that kept you from trusting them was never designed to protect you. It was designed to sell to you.
