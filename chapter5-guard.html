The National Guard

Understanding the dual federal-state authority of the National Guard and its routine operations

The National Guard's Long History

The National Guard has been federalized numerous times throughout American history, and this is not a phenomenon unique to any single president. Derek Johnson's claim that Guard federalization under Trump is somehow unprecedented or evidence of a secret military operation is flatly contradicted by the historical record.

The National Guard has been federalized for every major conflict the United States has engaged in, including:

  • World War I: Guard units were federalized and deployed to Europe as part of the American Expeditionary Forces.
  • World War II: Guard divisions were among the first units mobilized after Pearl Harbor, serving in every theater of operations.
  • Korean War: Multiple Guard divisions and smaller units were called to federal service.
  • Vietnam War: Guard units were activated and deployed throughout the conflict.
  • Persian Gulf War (1990–1991): Over 75,000 Guard members were called to active duty.
  • Iraq and Afghanistan (2001–2021): The largest sustained Guard mobilization since World War II, with hundreds of thousands of Guard members deploying over two decades.

This extensive history of federalization completely destroys Derek Johnson's claim that Guard federalization is unique to Trump or evidence of a secret military operation. Federalization is a routine, well-documented, legally authorized process that has been used by presidents of both parties for over a century.

Active-Guard Reserve (AGR)

The Active-Guard Reserve (AGR) program is a full-time military program that has existed since the 1970s. AGR members serve on full-time duty while remaining part of the National Guard (reserve component), providing the day-to-day operational backbone that keeps Guard units ready.

  • Established in the 1970s, the AGR program has been in continuous operation for over five decades.
  • Full-time duty status while remaining part of the reserve component (National Guard or Reserves)
  • Managed by the National Guard Bureau under both state and federal authorities
  • Dual role: AGR soldiers support both state missions (such as disaster response, emergency management, and training) and federal missions (such as overseas deployments and national defense)
  • Benefits similar to active-duty service members, including healthcare, housing allowances, and retirement benefits

The AGR program is not secret, unusual, or evidence of anything other than the routine administrative and operational needs of the National Guard. It exists because the Guard requires full-time personnel to manage training, equipment, logistics, and readiness. These are the same functions any military organization requires.

Title 32 vs Title 10: Know the Difference

Understanding the legal distinction between Title 32 and Title 10 status is essential to debunking claims about Guard activations. These two statuses define the chain of command, funding, and legal authorities under which Guard members operate.

Title 32 Status Title 10 Status
Under state governor command Under presidential/federal authority
Used for state emergencies Federalized for national missions
Exempt from Posse Comitatus Act Subject to Posse Comitatus Act
State funded or federal reimbursement Fully federal funded
Governor can deploy Only President can federalize
Derek Johnson claims National Guard activations prove a secret military operation. In reality, Guard activations happen routinely for natural disasters, civil unrest, border operations, pandemic response, inauguration security, and training exercises. None of this proves devolution.

Exercises: The Guard Trains Constantly

The National Guard participates in a wide range of training exercises throughout the year, both domestically and internationally. These exercises are planned well in advance, publicly documented, and have been running for years or even decades. They are not evidence of secret operations.

  • Vigilant Guard (2001–present): Multi-state disaster response exercise coordinated by U.S. Northern Command.
  • Global Guardian (2001–present): U.S. Strategic Command annual exercise testing nuclear command and control.
  • Northern Edge (1975–present): Alaska-based joint training exercise involving all service branches.
  • Cyber Shield (2012–present): National Guard cyber defense exercise testing network protection capabilities.
  • Golden Coyote (1984–present): South Dakota National Guard annual training exercise at Camp Rapid.
  • Northern Strike (2012–present): Michigan National Guard joint multinational exercise at Camp Grayling.
  • Yama Sakura (1982–present): U.S.-Japan bilateral command post exercise for alliance interoperability.
  • Key Resolve / Foal Eagle (2002–2018): U.S.-South Korea combined exercises for peninsula defense readiness.
  • Saber Strike (2010–2020): U.S. Army Europe multinational exercise in the Baltic region.
  • Foresight (2002–present): Emergency preparedness exercise testing Guard response capabilities.
Important Context: The National Guard Bureau's official account celebrated 30 years of the State Partnership Program. Any indication of the Guard performing anything other than long-standing programs is a false claim taken completely out of context.
