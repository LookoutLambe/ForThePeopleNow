Five Questions That Expose Every Grifter

You do not need a degree in emergency management or a military career to identify conspiracy content. You just need five questions.

1. What are their credentials?

Did this person serve in the military? In what capacity? For how long? What was their MOS? Did they have a security clearance? Did they study the topic professionally? A 14S National Guard soldier with 21 months of service is not a credible source on classified continuity protocols. A computer technician is not a military analyst. A woman with a diploma mill PhD is not an academic authority. Credentials matter.

2. Are they charging money?

If someone genuinely believed a secret military operation was underway to save the country, sharing that information freely would be a patriotic duty. Instead, they put it behind paywalls, sell books, charge for livestreams, and monetize every platform they operate on. If the information were real, it would be free. The paywall tells you everything you need to know about what they actually believe.

3. What is their track record?

How many of their predictions have come true? Mass arrests? Military tribunals? Emergency Broadcast System activation? Currency resets? If someone has made hundreds of predictions and zero have been fulfilled, that is not a source. That is a slot machine that never pays out but keeps taking your quarters.

4. Can you read the source document?

When someone claims an executive order proves something, can you read the executive order yourself? If yes, read it and compare what it says to what they claim. If they discourage you from reading the source document and insist you need their interpretation, that is a red flag. Real analysts point you to the source. Grifters point you to their Substack.

5. What happens when they are wrong?

When a prediction fails, do they acknowledge it and adjust? Or do they move the goalposts, blame the deep state, and make a new prediction? Real analysts admit when they are wrong because their credibility depends on accuracy. Grifters never admit they are wrong because their revenue depends on the narrative continuing regardless of results.
