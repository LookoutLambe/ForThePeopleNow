So What Is Constitutional Warfare?

The Constitution of the United States has been under attack since the day it was ratified. That is not a conspiracy theory. That is American history. Every generation has faced forces that tried to undermine, circumvent, or outright destroy the constitutional system. And every generation of Americans has had to fight back.

Constitutional warfare is the ongoing struggle over whether the principles in the Constitution actually mean what they say. It is not fought on a battlefield. It is fought in courtrooms, in legislatures, in the press, and now, on your phone. The difference today is that the attackers do not wear uniforms or hold office. They run Substacks, Telegram channels, and paid membership sites. They wrap themselves in the flag while asking you to abandon the document that flag represents.

Think about that for a moment. The grifters covered in this site claim to be patriots. They sell merchandise with American flags on it. They cite the Constitution constantly. And yet every single one of their theories requires the Constitution to have been secretly overridden, suspended, or replaced. That is not patriotism. The Constitution Has Survived Everything

Before we talk about today's threats, look at what the Constitution has already survived. Not theories about threats. Actual, documented, historical threats that were far more dangerous than anything a Substack blogger has ever produced:

1798
The Alien and Sedition Acts
Just seven years after the Bill of Rights, President John Adams signed laws making it illegal to criticize the government. The Constitution survived. The laws expired. Adams lost re-election. The system corrected itself.
1861-1865
The Civil War
Eleven states tried to leave the Union. President Lincoln suspended habeas corpus. Over 600,000 Americans died. The Constitution survived, and came out stronger with the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments that abolished slavery and guaranteed equal protection. The Reconstruction-era legislation that followed is covered in Chapter 8, where we show how the 1871 Act was part of this process, not a secret corporate takeover.
1942
Japanese American Internment
Executive Order 9066 forced 120,000 Japanese Americans into concentration camps. The Supreme Court shamefully upheld it. The Constitution survived, and decades later the government formally apologized and paid reparations, acknowledging the violation. The system corrected itself. It took too long, but it corrected.
1950s
McCarthyism
Senator Joseph McCarthy accused thousands of Americans of being communists with little or no evidence. Careers were destroyed. Lives were ruined. Sound familiar? The Constitution survived. McCarthy was censured by the Senate and his name became synonymous with baseless accusations. Today's conspiracy promoters are tomorrow's McCarthys.
1972-1974
Watergate
President Nixon authorized illegal surveillance and tried to cover it up. When the evidence came out, the constitutional system worked exactly as designed: Congress investigated, the Supreme Court ordered the tapes released, and Nixon resigned rather than face impeachment. No secret military plan was needed. The Constitution handled it.
2001
The Patriot Act
After 9/11, fear drove the passage of sweeping surveillance powers. The Homeland Security Act of 2002 created an entirely new department. Edward Snowden later exposed mass domestic surveillance. The Constitution survived. Courts struck down portions of the program, and public debate led to reforms. Again, no secret plan. The system corrected itself.
Every one of these was a real constitutional crisis, not an anonymous internet theory. In every case, the system the Founders built corrected itself. Not quickly. Not perfectly. But it corrected. The Constitution does not need a secret military plan to protect it. It has built-in mechanisms: the courts, the Congress, the press, and the people. You are one of those people. That is why you are reading this.
