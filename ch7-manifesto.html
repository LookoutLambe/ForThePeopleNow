CHAPTER 7: SUPPLEMENT

The "Anti-Fascist Manifesto"

Derek Johnson wrote a document calling military rule of civilians the cure for fascism. Let's read it together.
Before you continue: This page is a supplement to Chapter 7: The Law of War Theory, which documents Derek Johnson's credentials (21 months, E-4, National Guard, no security clearance, no operational experience). If you haven't read that chapter yet, start there. The manifesto makes more sense — and makes less sense — once you know who wrote it.

Who Wrote This, and Why Does It Have That Title?

Derek Johnson — RattleTrap 1776 — published a document he titled "The Antifascist Manifesto." That title is doing a lot of heavy lifting for an E-4 who spent 21 months in the National Guard before a medical separation.

The word "antifascist" is a deliberate rhetorical move. It frames what Johnson is selling — military control of civilian government — as resistance to tyranny rather than an example of it. If you can get someone to believe that a soldier seizing power from elected officials is actually fighting fascism, you have done something genuinely impressive. Impressive in the same way a card trick is impressive. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

The original document circulated through his Telegram channels and appeared on the old ForThePeople website as a downloadable .docx file. The old site linked to it without analysis. We're going to fix that.

The Core Claim: Military Rule Is Freedom

Johnson's manifesto rests on one foundational idea: the United States is currently under fascist occupation by a globalist cabal, and therefore the military has both the right and the obligation to intervene — outside of normal constitutional channels — to restore the republic.

Read that slowly.

His solution to the threat of authoritarian government is authoritarian government. His cure for the disease is a stronger dose of the same pathogen. The manifesto calls for military forces to override civilian elections, civilian courts, and civilian authority — and labels this "antifascist."

The textbook definition of fascism includes militarism, the subordination of civil institutions to military authority, and the belief that the nation faces an existential enemy that justifies emergency rule. Derek Johnson's manifesto checks every one of those boxes — while calling itself the opposite.

This is not a new trick. Every coup in history has been announced as liberation. Every strongman has had a story about why this time civilian government needs to step aside. The Roman Senate gave Julius Caesar emergency powers to save the Republic. That did not work out well for the Republic.

The Three Pillars of the Manifesto

The manifesto builds its case on three claims, each of which has a factual answer.

Pillar 1: The Law of War Manual Authorizes Military Governance

Johnson claims the DoD Law of War Manual gives the military authority to operate as a governing body over the civilian population during a period of "enemy occupation" — and that this authority was invoked by Trump before he left office.
The Law of War Manual (DoD 12473.13-M) governs how U.S. military forces conduct operations in foreign theaters of war. It applies to occupied foreign territory — places like post-WWII Germany or post-2003 Iraq. It does not apply to American soil. It has never applied to American soil. Applying it domestically would violate the Posse Comitatus Act (18 U.S.C. § 1385), the Insurrection Act, the Constitution itself, and approximately two centuries of settled American law. The manual literally says so. Johnson never quotes that part.

For the full document analysis with citations, see Chapter 7: The Law of War Manual.

Pillar 2: Executive Orders Created a Secret Transfer of Power

Johnson argues that a series of Trump executive orders — particularly EO 13848, EO 13912, and EO 13961 — combined with the Military Justice Act of 2016, created a legal framework under which Trump transferred authority to the military before Biden's inauguration.
Each of these executive orders has been read by the people who study them professionally — attorneys, emergency management officials, and constitutional scholars. None of them contain language that transfers executive authority to the military. EO 13848 addresses foreign interference in elections. EO 13912 activated National Guard resources during COVID-19. EO 13961 updated continuity of government planning for pandemic conditions. These are routine policy instruments. The Military Justice Act of 2016 modernized the Uniform Code of Military Justice. It does not grant the military governing authority over civilians. Ever.
Here is a useful test: when someone claims a document says something, read the document. Every executive order Johnson cites is publicly available and linked in Chapter 7. If the secret transfer of power were in those documents, you would be able to find it. Thousands of lawyers, journalists, and government officials have read them. No one has found it. The simplest explanation is that it is not there.

Pillar 3: Americans Who Disagree Are Enemy Collaborators

This is the pillar the manifesto is most careful about — but it's there. Once you accept that the United States is under enemy occupation, anyone who defends the current civilian government becomes, by definition, a collaborator with the occupying enemy.

Johnson has called Americans who disagree with his framework "lamebrains." He has framed critics as people who "don't want to be free." He has suggested that those who accept the 2020 election results are either blind, corrupt, or complicit.

This is where the manifesto becomes genuinely dangerous. Not because one man with 21 months of National Guard service is going to overthrow the government. But because a large audience that has been told their neighbors are enemy agents is an audience that has been primed for something much worse than bad civics.

The Irony That Deserves Its Own Section

Derek Johnson is a man who:

  • Believes civilian elections should be overridden by military authority
  • Believes civilian courts that ruled against his preferred candidate are corrupt
  • Believes civilian media that contradicts his narrative is enemy propaganda
  • Believes the solution to all of this is military command of the government
  • Has named this position "antifascism"

Benito Mussolini defined fascism as the merger of state and corporate power under military-nationalist ideology, with civilian institutions subordinated to military and executive authority. Derek Johnson's manifesto is, by most standard political science definitions, a fascist document. He has simply put "anti" in front of the word and pointed in a different direction.

"Fascism should more properly be called corporatism, since it is the merger of state and corporate power." — Benito Mussolini

"When fascism comes to America, it will be wrapped in the flag and carrying a cross." — Often attributed to Sinclair Lewis, It Can't Happen Here (1935)

We are not calling Derek Johnson a fascist. We are pointing out that his manifesto advocates for the subordination of civilian government to military authority, calls critics of this position enemies of the republic, and frames this entire package as liberation. These are the structural ingredients of fascism regardless of the flag on the uniform.

What the Constitution Actually Says About This

The framers were not naive. They had lived under British military occupation. They knew what it looked like when soldiers governed civilians. That experience is baked into the founding documents with unusual specificity.

📄

Article II, Section 1

Executive power is vested in the President of the United States. It transfers to a new president upon inauguration. There is no mechanism — none — for that transfer to be paused, redirected to the military, or held in escrow pending some future "storm."

⚖

The Third Amendment

No soldier shall be quartered in any house without the consent of the owner. The framers put this in the Bill of Rights because British military occupation of civilian homes was one of their specific grievances. The entire Constitution is structured to prevent what Johnson calls a solution.

⚖

Posse Comitatus Act (1878)

Federal law. 18 U.S.C. § 1385. Prohibits the use of federal military personnel to enforce domestic civilian law. Passed specifically because Americans had seen what happened during Reconstruction when soldiers acted as police. This law is not a technicality. It is a firewall.

📄

The Military Oath

Every officer swears to "support and defend the Constitution of the United States against all enemies, foreign and domestic" and to "bear true faith and allegiance to the same." The oath is to the Constitution — not to a president, a party, or a plan. The Joint Chiefs made this explicit on January 12, 2021. Chapter 2 covers the oath in full.

Where Did This Document Come From?

Johnson published the manifesto during the peak of his online influence, roughly 2022 through 2023, when his "blueprint" framework was pulling in large numbers of followers across Telegram and TruthSocial. The document was designed to give his audience something to share — a single downloadable file that packaged his worldview with a provocative title.

The old ForThePeople website hosted it without commentary because the goal at that time was to gather everything in one place. That was the right instinct. The missing piece was the analysis you're reading now.

The document circulated widely through patriot communities and was often cited as proof that Johnson's claims had a serious intellectual foundation. It does not have a serious intellectual foundation. It has a provocative title, some correctly cited document numbers, and a conclusion that doesn't follow from any of its premises.

What Actual Defense of Liberty Looks Like

If you are genuinely worried about the direction of this country — and there are legitimate reasons to be worried — the answer is not a man with a Telegram channel and 21 months of National Guard service telling you the military is secretly running things. The answer is the same thing it has always been.

  • Vote. In every election. Federal, state, local. School boards matter. County commissioners matter. Judges matter.
  • Run. If you're angry about who's on your ballot, get on the ballot yourself or recruit someone who shares your values.
  • Read the documents. Every document Johnson cites is publicly available. We've linked them throughout this site. The law is not hidden from you. Read it.
  • Know your rights. The First Amendment, the Fourth Amendment, the due process clause — these are your tools. They don't require a military rescue operation. They require citizens who know how to use them.
  • Recognize the grift. When someone tells you only they have the secret knowledge, only they can decode what's really happening, and oh by the way there's a Telegram subscription — that is a sales pitch, not a briefing.
The Constitution has survived 237 years, a Civil War, two World Wars, assassinations, recessions, the Cold War, and 9/11. It did not survive all of that because an E-4 with a manifesto was watching over it. It survived because enough Americans understood it, fought for it, and refused to trade it away for a story about a secret plan. Be one of those Americans.
