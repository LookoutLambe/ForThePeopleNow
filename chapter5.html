Who is Derek Johnson?

Derek Johnson has garnered significant attention on social media, largely owing to his frequent fixation on the Military Justice Act of 2016 and the Law of War Manual. He burst onto the scene around January 2022, citing military regulations to "prove" that President Trump remains the President of the United States. He claims this assertion is supported by laws and military regulations.

According to Johnson, the two documents that constitute his "blueprint" are the Military Justice Act of 2016 and the Department of Defense (DoD) Law of War Manual. He positions these documents as a foundational cornerstone of his claims about the current state of the U.S. government and military operations.

When someone with a Master's in Emergency Management reads the documents Derek Johnson cites, the errors aren't subtle. He gets basic historical facts wrong. He confuses unrelated laws. He mischaracterizes one of the most important Supreme Court cases in American history. These are not matters of interpretation — they are factual errors.

While Johnson presents himself as an authority on military law and government operations, it's essential to approach his claims with a critical eye. His background doesn't include formal military legal education or emergency management training. As with any source of information, especially those making extraordinary claims about government operations, we encourage you to cross-reference his assertions and scrutinize the evidence behind the narrative.

The Military Justice Act of 2016

The Military Justice Act refers to the United States Military Justice System and the legislative framework governing military justice in the U.S. The Military Justice Act of 2016, which took effect January 2019, introduced reforms and updates to the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ), which is the foundation of military law in the United States.

What the Act actually did:

  • Modernized and streamlined the military justice system with new provisions related to investigations, court-martial procedures, and sentencing
  • Increased rights and protections for victims of military crimes, including sexual assault and domestic violence
  • Eliminated some offenses from the UCMJ and created new ones to address contemporary issues like cybercrimes
  • Introduced changes to sentencing guidelines and appellate review processes
  • Enhanced the role of military judges in pre-trial proceedings
Derek Johnson treats the Military Justice Act as evidence of a secret military operation and conflates it with the National Emergencies Act (1976) and Executive Order 12148 (1979), treating them as interchangeable.
These are three completely different legal instruments. The MJA modernized court-martial procedures. The NEA (signed by Gerald Ford, 1976) establishes procedures for emergency declarations. EO 12148 (signed by Jimmy Carter, 1979) transferred emergency management to FEMA. Different purposes, different years, different authors.
Johnson implies the UCMJ predates the Declaration of Independence, suggesting military law has a continuous, unbroken history supporting his framework.
The UCMJ was enacted by Congress in 1950. While military law existed since the Articles of War (1775), the UCMJ as a unified code is a 20th century product. Johnson is off by about 175 years. This is a foundational error in his entire argument.

The Department of Defense Law of War Manual

The DoD Law of War Manual serves as the Department of Defense's comprehensive guide on the law of war. It provides legal guidance for U.S. military personnel on the conduct of armed conflict. Understanding its purpose is essential when evaluating claims that reference it:

  • Purpose: It provides legal guidance for the conduct of armed conflict — meaning wars with foreign adversaries, not domestic governance
  • Legal framework: It outlines the legal principles, rules, and guidelines governing the use of force in armed conflict
  • Scope: Covers international law, treatment of prisoners, rules of engagement, targeting, and humanitarian protections
  • Audience: Written for military personnel engaged in armed conflict operations, not for domestic law enforcement or governance
The Law of War Manual governs how the U.S. military conducts itself during wartime against foreign adversaries. It does not authorize or describe domestic military occupation of the United States. Applying it to domestic governance represents a fundamental misunderstanding of its purpose and scope.
Johnson claims Marbury v. Madison (1803) supports his idea that laws may not be as they "appear," suggesting a hidden legal reality.
Marbury v. Madison established judicial review — the Supreme Court's power to declare acts of Congress unconstitutional. Chief Justice Marshall's opinion established that the Constitution is the supreme law of the land. Johnson's characterization bears zero resemblance to the actual case. This is Constitutional Law 101.

Fact Checking Derek Johnson's Claims

Derek Johnson's website, 1776 Nation, features a comprehensive list of information, accompanied by an encouragement for visitors to fact-check the content provided. We took him up on that offer.

Several Government Employees, Veterans, and Active Duty personnel have reviewed his claims and provided numerous examples proving his main points to be inaccurate. Here are the main arguments we address with factual information using verified sources and decades of experience, knowledge, and training:

Derek Johnson Claims The Verified Facts
The U.S. is in a Continuity of Government COG plans exist for emergencies but are not currently "activated" in the way Johnson describes. See Chapter 6 for full details.
The National Guard has only been federalized by President Trump National Guard federalization has happened under many presidents for disasters, civil unrest, and emergencies. See National Guard page.
The Secretary of Defense delegated authority proves a military operation Authority delegation is standard administrative procedure in any large organization. The DoD has published directives on this since its inception.
The Law of War Manual applies to current domestic operations The Manual governs conduct during wartime with foreign adversaries. It does not authorize domestic military occupation.
Executive orders contain hidden instructions for military takeover The cited EOs update existing continuity frameworks maintained across all administrations. Read EO 13848 yourself.

On page 24 of his materials, Johnson references 50 United States Code 34, directing to Chapter 2, Section 1622 about the termination of declared national emergencies. According to the legal framework, a national emergency can be terminated through two avenues:

  1. A joint resolution passed by Congress discontinuing the emergency
  2. The President issuing a proclamation officially terminating the emergency

Additionally, the President is required to transmit to Congress an annual notice of intent to continue any existing national emergencies. What Johnson fails to explain is that these are routine administrative processes that have been used across every modern presidency — they are not evidence of a secret military operation.

Derek Johnson's Own Contradictions

When you look at Johnson's own statements side by side, contradictions emerge that undermine his entire framework. Since he encourages fact-checking, let's take him at his word:

Contradiction 1: Johnson says "This isn't about me, period" but then states "I've taken the Laws and Orders that are different than any other time in History" — implying the interpretation revolves entirely around him.
If the laws speak for themselves, why does the interpretation require Derek Johnson specifically? Either the documents are self-evident or they need his unique interpretation. He claims both simultaneously.
Contradiction 2: Johnson declares "There are not any Laws and Orders that rescind, revise, or revoke" the operation, but also says "I'm simply taking Laws and Orders I didn't write or pass and interpreting them."
If he's merely "interpreting" documents he didn't write, his interpretation could be wrong. But he simultaneously claims the operation is unstoppable and beyond revision — a conclusion that goes far beyond interpretation.
Contradiction 3: "There's nothing in the world that has stopped, will stop, or can stop what's happening" conflicts with his claim to simply be interpreting documents objectively.
An objective interpreter doesn't make absolute predictions about unstoppable outcomes. This language reveals that Johnson has moved from analysis to prophecy — a hallmark of conspiracy thinking, not research.

Continuity of Government: The Real Story

Johnson's claims about Continuity of Government (COG) represent perhaps the most dangerous misunderstanding in his framework. He portrays COG as evidence of a secret military operation, when in reality it's a well-documented, publicly available set of emergency preparedness plans.

For the complete breakdown of what COG actually is, how it works, and why it disproves the devolution and law of war theories, see Chapter 6: Continuity of Government — the most comprehensive chapter on this site.

COG plans are designed to preserve the constitutional order during emergencies, not overthrow it. Every COG document explicitly requires maintaining the separation of powers and the rule of law. This is the opposite of what Derek Johnson claims.
